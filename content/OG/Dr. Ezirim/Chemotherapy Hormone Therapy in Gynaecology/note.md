## Chemotherapy — definition and tumour growth

**Chemotherapy** is the treatment of disease using **chemical substances**. The **ideal agent selectively kills tumour cells with minimal toxicity to normal tissues**.

**Tumour growth:** cells progress through the **cell cycle** to divide; the **generation time** is the time to complete one cycle.

> **Tumour cells do NOT have a faster generation time than normal cells.** However, tumours have **more cells in the active phases of replication**, so they generally grow faster. Chemotherapy exploits this difference to preferentially kill tumour cells.

The **doubling time** is the time for a tumour mass to double in size; it varies widely, and **metastases generally have faster doubling times than the primary lesion**.

## Mechanisms of drug action

| Class | Examples | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Alkylating agents** | Melphalan, ifosfamide, cyclophosphamide | **Cross-link DNA strands**, preventing protein synthesis and cell division |
| **Antimetabolites** | Methotrexate, 5-fluorouracil | **Inhibit metabolic processes** required for DNA/RNA synthesis |
| **Anti-tumour antibiotics** | Dactinomycin, mitomycin, bleomycin, adriamycin (doxorubicin) | Natural products from soil fungi |
| **Plant alkaloids** | Etoposide, vinblastine, vincristine, taxol | **Arrest cells in metaphase** by binding microtubular protein of the mitotic spindle |
| **Miscellaneous** | Cisplatin, carboplatin, hexamethylmelamine | Do not fit the above categories |

## Pharmacological considerations & toxicity

- Chemotherapy works by **first-order kinetics** — killing a **constant FRACTION** of cells, not a constant number
- Most agents are given **intravenously**, and eliminated by the **kidneys** or metabolised in the **liver**
- **Drug toxicity** = unwanted effects from injury to normal cells; it **limits the dose**
- Agents are used at doses producing **some toxicity** to be maximally effective
- Toxicity is quantified by the **therapeutic index** (ratio of therapeutic dose to toxic dose)
- **Haematologic toxicity** (leukopenia, thrombocytopenia, anaemia) is the **most frequent**; also **GI** (mucositis, diarrhoea) and **skin** (alopecia)

## Clinical remission

- **Complete remission** — disappearance of **all** objective evidence of tumour and complete resolution of all signs and symptoms
- **Partial remission** — a reduction of **at least 50%** in all measurable lesions, some subjective improvement, and **no new lesions** during therapy

## Drug resistance

A major problem. By the **Goldie-Coldman hypothesis**, most cells start **sensitive** but develop **resistance at variable rates**.

> **Tumours are only curable if no resistant cell lines are present or develop.** To minimise resistance, **multiple-drug regimens are preferred to single-drug therapy.**

**Pleiotropic (multidrug) resistance** — certain resistance mechanisms confer **cross-resistance to structurally dissimilar drugs** with different mechanisms.

## Principles of clinical chemotherapy

Before starting:

- **Histological classification** of the tumour is essential
- Determine the **therapeutic index** relative to the **stage**
- Evaluate the role of chemotherapy/hormones vs other modalities
- Assess **patient factors** — age, performance status, organ reserve, comorbidity
- **Start with the most effective agent** (cheaper long-term, avoids prolonged hospitalisation)
- Ensure **infrastructure** — trained personnel and sterile mixing facilities

> **Summary of successful drug therapy:** an **effective agent** at the **best dose/schedule**; a tumour with a **high growth fraction**, **accessible** to drugs (not in a sanctuary), with **small volume or large fractional kill** (to avoid resistant clones); and **normal tissue that recovers faster than the tumour regenerates.**

## Chemotherapy in gynaecological cancers

**Ovarian cancer** — responds **best** of all gynaecological cancers; chemotherapy is **standard after initial surgery**. First-line: **platinum (cisplatin/carboplatin) + paclitaxel**. If hypersensitive to paclitaxel: cyclophosphamide or topotecan. **Intraperitoneal chemotherapy** delivers sustained high doses to the peritoneum while sparing marrow. **Malignant germ-cell tumours:** platinum-based **BEP (bleomycin, etoposide, cisplatin)**.

**Endometrial cancer** — chemotherapy mainly for **advanced/metastatic/recurrent** disease. Most active: **doxorubicin, platinum, paclitaxel.** **Medroxyprogesterone acetate (MPA)** 200–400 mg/day gives ~40% response but is not valuable as an adjuvant.

**Cervical cancer (squamous)** — increasingly shown sensitive to cytostatics. **Chemoradiation** (chemotherapy + concurrent radiotherapy) is now an important modality. Most effective agents: **cisplatin, or cisplatin + 5-FU.** Platinum-based chemoradiation gives a **~30% reduction in risk of death** in locally advanced disease vs radiotherapy alone.

**Vaginal cancer** — chemotherapy (including cisplatin) has **no significant activity**.

**Vulval cancer** — used in **advanced, unresectable** disease: **mitomycin C + 5-FU with external beam radiotherapy.**

## Gestational trophoblastic disease (GTD)

**Indications for chemotherapy:**

- **β-hCG > 20,000 IU/ml persisting 4 weeks** after evacuation
- **Rising β-hCG**
- **Histological choriocarcinoma**
- **Metastasis**

Patients are categorised by the **WHO scoring system** (age, antecedent pregnancy, β-hCG level, interval, tumour size, site and number of metastases, prior chemotherapy — each scored 0–4):

| Risk | Score | Regimen |
|---|---|---|
| **Low** | 0–4 | **Methotrexate** alone (± folinic acid) or **actinomycin D** |
| **Medium** | 5–7 | Combination — **EMA-CO** (etoposide, methotrexate, actinomycin D, cyclophosphamide, vincristine) |
| **High** | > 8 | **EMA-CO**; if it fails, cisplatin/etoposide/bleomycin/ifosfamide |

## Side effects of chemotherapy (drug-specific)

- **Nausea & vomiting** — metoclopramide, dexamethasone, lorazepam, 5-HT antagonists
- **Bone marrow depression** — almost all agents
- **Phlebitis/sclerosant** — especially **adriamycin**
- **Neurologic** — **bleomycin, vincristine**
- **Skin exfoliation** — **bleomycin**
- **Pulmonary** — **bleomycin, methotrexate**
- **Nephrotoxicity** — **cisplatin** (less with carboplatin)
- **Hepatotoxicity** — **methotrexate**
- **Cardiotoxicity** — **adriamycin (doxorubicin)**
- **GIT** — methotrexate, 5-FU, cyclophosphamide
- **Alopecia** — almost all
- **Stomatitis** — methotrexate, 5-FU, adriamycin

## Hormone therapy

**Hormones** are chemical substances produced by one organ that act on distant **target organs**. Indications in gynaecology:

- **Hormonal contraceptives** — Oral (COCs, progestin-only); injectables (DMPA, Noristerat); implants (Norplant, Uniplant, LNG-IUS); **post-coital (Yuzpe regimen** — 2 pills of 50 µg ethinylestradiol + 0.5 mg norgestrel within 72 h, repeated 12 h later); IUD insertion or mifepristone
- **Hormone replacement therapy (HRT)** — vasomotor symptoms (hot flushes; **oestradiol** mainly); **osteoporosis** (the most important menopausal hazard — lowest effective dose, e.g. 0.625 mg conjugated oestradiol); genitourinary atrophy; **hypogonadism** (sexual maturation, epiphyseal closure); premature ovarian failure; hypothalamic-pituitary suppression (exercise-induced amenorrhoea, anorexia nervosa)
- **Infertility / assisted reproduction**, **pregnancy maintenance**, **abnormal uterine bleeding**, **hyperandrogenism/hirsutism**, gynae-oncology/endometriosis, and miscellaneous (e.g. urethral mucosal prolapse)
