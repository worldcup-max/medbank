# Chemotherapy in Gynaecology — the simple version

## The one idea chemotherapy is built on

> Cancer cells aren't *faster* than normal cells — there are just **more of them dividing at once.**

Chemo drugs attack **dividing cells**. Since a tumour has a bigger fraction of cells dividing, chemo hits the tumour **harder than normal tissue** — but it still hits normal dividing tissue (marrow, gut lining, hair), which is exactly why the side effects are what they are.

> **Hold this: chemo kills DIVIDING cells → tumours suffer most, but marrow/gut/hair suffer too.**

---

## How the drugs work — 5 families

Don't memorise every drug; learn the **family and its trick:**

- **Alkylating agents** (cyclophosphamide, ifosfamide) → **glue the DNA strands together** so the cell can't copy them
- **Antimetabolites** (methotrexate, 5-FU) → **block the building blocks** for making DNA/RNA
- **Anti-tumour antibiotics** (bleomycin, adriamycin) → natural fungal products
- **Plant alkaloids** (vincristine, etoposide, taxol) → **freeze the cell in metaphase** by jamming the mitotic spindle
- **Miscellaneous** (cisplatin, carboplatin) → the platinums, don't fit a box

---

## Two numbers that matter

**First-order kinetics:**
> Chemo kills a constant **FRACTION**, not a constant **number.** So each dose removes (say) 99% of what's there — never the last cell in one go. That's why you need **repeated cycles.**

**Therapeutic index:**
> The gap between the **helpful dose** and the **toxic dose.** A bigger gap = safer drug.

**And the most common toxicity to remember:**
> **Bone marrow (blood) toxicity** — leukopenia, thrombocytopenia, anaemia — is the **commonest** side effect (because marrow is a fast-dividing tissue).

---

## Why one drug is rarely enough — resistance

**Goldie-Coldman idea:**
> Cells start **sensitive** but **develop resistance** over time.

So:
> **Use MULTIPLE drugs together.** One tumour cell is unlikely to be resistant to several different mechanisms at once. Single-drug therapy lets resistant clones take over.

**Pleiotropic (multidrug) resistance** = a cell becomes resistant to **many unrelated drugs** at once — the nightmare scenario.

---

## Which gynae cancers respond?

Rank them by how well chemo works:

> **Ovarian = responds BEST** → chemo is standard **after surgery.** First-line: **platinum + paclitaxel.**
>
> **Cervix (squamous)** → **chemoradiation** (cisplatin ± 5-FU with radiotherapy).
>
> **Endometrial** → mostly for **advanced/recurrent** disease.
>
> **Vulva** → advanced/unresectable, with radiotherapy.
>
> **Vagina** → chemo has **almost NO activity** (the exception to remember).

**Two drug-combo names worth knowing:**
- **BEP** (Bleomycin, Etoposide, Platinum/cisplatin) → ovarian **germ-cell** tumours
- **EMA-CO** → gestational trophoblastic disease (medium/high risk)

---

## GTD — the exam favourite

When do you give chemo for gestational trophoblastic disease? Four triggers:

> **β-hCG > 20,000 four weeks after evacuation · rising β-hCG · choriocarcinoma on histology · metastasis.**

Then you score risk (WHO) and pick the regimen:
> **Low risk → methotrexate** (single agent). **Medium/high → EMA-CO** (combination).

> **The single most memorable line: low-risk GTD = methotrexate; higher-risk = EMA-CO.**

---

## Side effects — learn the DRUG-SPECIFIC ones

Everyone gets marrow suppression and hair loss. The exam tests the **drug-specific** toxicities — learn these pairs:

> **Cisplatin → kidneys** (nephrotoxic)
> **Adriamycin (doxorubicin) → heart** (cardiotoxic)
> **Bleomycin → lungs** (pulmonary fibrosis)
> **Methotrexate → liver** (and lungs, GIT, mouth)
> **Vincristine → nerves** (neuropathy)

**Memory hook:** cisPLATin = kidney (platinum through kidneys); adriamycin = the "A" of heart Attack; **B**leomycin = **B**reathing (lungs).

---

## Hormone therapy — the other half

Hormones (made in one organ, act on a distant target) are used all over gynaecology:

> **Contraception, HRT, infertility/IVF, pregnancy support, abnormal bleeding, hirsutism, oncology.**

**HRT high-yield points:**
> **Hot flushes → oestrogen.** **Osteoporosis** is the **most important menopausal hazard** — use the **lowest effective dose.**

**Emergency contraception:**
> **Yuzpe regimen** — 2 combined pills within **72 hours**, repeated 12 hours later.

---

## Putting it all together

Chemo attacks **dividing cells**, so tumours (with more dividing cells) suffer most — but so do **marrow, gut and hair** (hence the side effects, marrow toxicity commonest).

Learn the **5 drug families** by their trick, use **combinations** to beat resistance (Goldie-Coldman), and know that chemo works **best in ovarian cancer**, via **chemoradiation** in cervix, and **not at all** in vaginal cancer.

For **GTD**: chemo if **β-hCG > 20,000 at 4 weeks, rising hCG, choriocarcinoma, or metastasis** — **methotrexate for low risk, EMA-CO for higher.**

And the drug-specific toxicities are the exam gold: **cisplatin-kidney, adriamycin-heart, bleomycin-lung, methotrexate-liver, vincristine-nerves.**
