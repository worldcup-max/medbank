# Blood Transfusion in O&G — the simple version

## Why this matters so much in O&G

> In tropical obstetrics, **haemorrhage is the #1 killer of mothers.**

So blood transfusion is a core life-saving skill — but with **HIV and donor shortages**, the rule is:

> **Transfuse only to save the mother's life** — not casually.

And the biggest recurring criticism in maternal deaths: transfusion given **"too little, too late."** The lesson: **high-risk women (likely to lose > 1 litre) should deliver where blood and ICU are available.**

---

## The big shift: components, not whole blood

> One donated unit is **split** into parts — **red cells, platelets, FFP, cryoprecipitate.**

**Why this is better:**
- Give **only what's needed** → better treatment
- **One unit treats several patients** → conserves scarce blood

> **Whole blood is now rarely used. Component therapy is the standard.**

---

## Match the component to the problem

Think: *what is the patient short of?*

| Give this | When they lack... |
|---|---|
| **Red cells** | oxygen-carrying capacity (anaemia) |
| **Platelets** | platelets (number or function) → bleeding |
| **FFP** | clotting factors (general) |
| **Cryoprecipitate** | **fibrinogen, factor VIII, XIII, vWF, fibronectin** |

> **Memory hook for cryoprecipitate:** it's the **fibrinogen/vWF/factor VIII** product. FFP is the *general* clotting-factor product.

---

## When do you transfuse? (indications)

> **Hb < 5.6 g/dl · symptomatic anaemia (incl. heart failure) · anaemia near term or in labour · acute loss > 30% of blood volume (or > 500 ml) not fixed by fluids.**

Notice the theme: it's about **being close to delivery** (least reserve for bleeding) or **actively losing a lot of blood.**

---

## The autologous family (using the patient's OWN blood)

Three ways to give someone back their own blood:

**1. Pre-op donation** — donate ahead of time.
> Needs **Hb ≥ 11**, **2 weeks before**. **NOT routine before delivery** (few mothers actually need it). Exception: **placenta praevia.**

**2. Intraoperative cell salvage (IOCS)** — collect the blood they're losing during surgery, wash it, give it back (a **"Cell Saver"** machine).
> **Contraindicated** with **infection, cancer cells, or bowel injury** (you'd re-inject contamination). Old "scoop and sieve" is banned.

**3. Acute normovolaemic haemodilution** — take blood at the start of surgery, replace with fluid, give the blood back at the end.
> Clever logic: **they bleed diluted blood during surgery (losing fewer red cells), and get their concentrated blood back after.**

---

## Exchange transfusion — know the one indication

> **Fastest way to correct anaemia, but expensive, laborious, rarely used.**
>
> **Only indication:** severe anaemia needing **very rapid** correction — e.g. **severe anaemia in early labour.**

---

## The safety rules (general principles + administration)

These are the "don't kill your patient" rules — high-yield:

> **Check group + antibodies at booking AND 28 weeks.**
> **Give ABO, RhD and Kell (K) compatible blood.**
> **In an emergency: O RhD-negative, K-negative** first, then switch to group-specific.
> **CMV-negative blood** for pregnancy/intrauterine transfusion.

**At the bedside:**
> **Two people check the unit** — the #1 danger is the **wrong blood to the wrong patient.**
> **Only NORMAL SALINE** in the same line — other fluids cause **haemolysis**; **calcium fluids clot** the tubing.
> **Never add drugs** to blood.

---

## What goes wrong (adverse effects) — learn the reaction types

**The dangerous one — ACUTE HAEMOLYTIC reaction:**
> Almost always **ABO incompatibility → intravascular haemolysis.**
> Signs: **chills, fever, chest & flank pain, collapse, DIC.**
> This is the "wrong blood" catastrophe — hence the double-checking.

**The common, mild one — FEBRILE NON-HAEMOLYTIC:**
> Antibodies against **white cells/platelets.** Just **fever.** Treat with **antipyretics**; use **leucocyte-reduced** blood if it keeps happening.

**The sneaky, late one — DELAYED HAEMOLYTIC:**
> From **Kidd, Duffy, Kell** groups. **Extravascular**, appears **7–10 days later** with fever, anaemia, jaundice, **positive direct Coombs.**

**Others:** infections (HIV, hepatitis, **CMV**), allergy, and **circulatory overload** (too much, too fast).

> **Quick sort:** immediate + collapse + chest/flank pain = **ABO acute haemolytic**. Just fever = **febrile non-haemolytic**. Days later + jaundice + Coombs+ = **delayed haemolytic**.

---

## Doing it well: Patient Blood Management (PBM)

The modern philosophy — **avoid transfusion by planning ahead:**
> **Optimise Hb before delivery (iron!), don't over-transfuse, use cell salvage, accept lower triggers, give iron to the stable non-bleeding woman.**

---

## The special case: Jehovah's Witnesses

They refuse blood — so plan **early and sensitively:**
> **Multidisciplinary plan · early (IV) iron · discuss and document refusal antenatally · use drugs/surgery to avoid banked blood · inform them of the real risks — without coercion.**

---

## Putting it all together

In O&G, **haemorrhage kills mothers**, so transfusion is life-saving — but used **only when needed**, and increasingly as **components, not whole blood** (match the deficiency: red cells, platelets, FFP, cryoprecipitate).

Transfuse for **Hb < 5.6, symptomatic anaemia, or major loss.** Use the patient's **own blood** where possible (pre-op donation, **cell salvage**, haemodilution).

The safety non-negotiables: **check group at booking and 28 weeks, give ABO/RhD/Kell-compatible (O-neg in emergency), two-person check, saline only, no drugs in the bag.**

Know the three reactions — **acute haemolytic (ABO, immediate, dangerous), febrile non-haemolytic (fever, mild), delayed haemolytic (Kidd/Duffy/Kell, 7–10 days, Coombs+)** — and the modern approach: **prevent transfusion with iron and planning (PBM)**, and handle **Jehovah's Witnesses** with early planning and documented consent.
