# Pneumonia — the simple version

## Start with the definition, word by word

> **Pneumonia** = inflammation of the **parenchyma** of the lung **caused by microorganisms**.

Two halves. Both matter.

**"Parenchyma"** = the working tissue. Not the pipes — the **gas exchange** part:

- Respiratory bronchioles
- Alveolar ducts
- Alveolar sacs
- Alveoli
- Interstitial tissue

**"Caused by microorganisms"** = a **germ** did it.

---

### The trap question

> If a child inhales kerosene and the lung becomes inflamed, is that pneumonia?

**No.** No germ.

That is **pneumonitis** (or alveolitis) — inflammation of the lung from **chemical or physical injury**.

> **Germ → pneumonia. Chemical or burn → pneumonitis.** One word, and it is a free mark.

---

## Four ways to classify — and each answers a different question

| Classify by | The question it answers |
|---|---|
| **Origin** | *Where did the child catch it?* |
| **Distribution** | *What shape is it in the lung?* |
| **Type of organism** | *What kind of germ?* |
| **Actual organism** | *Which exact germ?* |

**Origin:** community-acquired · hospital-acquired · aspiration · immunocompromised

**Distribution:** **lobar/segmental** vs **bronchopneumonia**

**Organism type:** viral · bacterial · fungal · atypical · parasitic

---

## The two patterns — this is the heart of the topic

Everything else in this lecture makes more sense once you can see these two pictures.

---

### LOBAR pneumonia — one solid block

> **One or more lobes, or part of a lobe. Consolidation is virtually HOMOGENOUS.**

Homogenous = **all one piece**, solid, uniform.

Picture pouring cement into **one room** of a house. That room is now solid. The other rooms are fine.

---

### BRONCHOPNEUMONIA — scattered patches

> **PATCHY consolidation throughout a lobe or the lungs.**

Patchy = **spots here and there**, with normal lung in between.

Picture splashing cement **randomly all over the house**. No room is completely full, but nowhere is completely clean either.

---

### Now everything follows from the shape

**Ask: if one area is SOLID, what happens to the signs there?**

Solid tissue **conducts sound better** and **is not resonant**.

| | Lobar | Bronchopneumonia |
|---|---|---|
| Shape | **one solid block** | **scattered patches** |
| Percussion | **DULL** over that area | **RESONANT, equal both sides** |
| Fremitus | **INCREASED** on that side | not a feature |
| Chest expansion | **DIMINISHED** on that side | not a feature |
| Breath sounds | diminished, then **BRONCHIAL** | diminished |
| Added sounds | crepitations later | **CREPITATIONS** |
| **Chest pain** | **PLEURITIC — present** | **UNUSUAL** |
| Trachea | **central** | **central** |

---

### Two things to hold onto

**1.** In **both** conditions the **trachea is CENTRAL**.

> If the trachea has shifted, this is **not** simple pneumonia. Think effusion, pneumothorax or collapse.

**2.** **Chest pain separates them.**

Lobar pneumonia reaches the **pleura**, so it hurts (pleuritic pain). Bronchopneumonia stays scattered in the middle of the lung, so **chest pain is unusual**.

---

## Who gets it, and why the numbers are shocking

- A child dies of pneumonia **every 43 seconds**
- **> 700,000** under-5 deaths in 2021 — about **2,000 a day**
- **15%** of ALL under-5 deaths
- **1 case per 71 children** every year

**Infants and toddlers** are hit hardest.

In Nigeria it peaks in the **rainy** and **harmattan** seasons.

---

### The risk factors — group them and they stop being a list

**The child is small or weak:**

- Age **under 2**
- **Low birth weight**
- **Malnutrition** (including **vitamin A deficiency**)
- **Previous illness** — HIV, sickle cell, congenital lung disease

**The child did not get protection:**

- **No breastfeeding**, especially not exclusive

**Too many germs around:**

- **Overcrowding**
- **Large family size**
- **Poor socio-economic status**

**The air is bad:**

- **Indoor and outdoor pollution**, smoking, wood smoke

> Notice how many of these are **preventable**. That is why the prevention slide is mostly health education.

---

## What causes it — the age rule

> **Viruses are the commonest cause overall.**
>
> **≈50%** at all ages. **Up to 80% in under-2s.**

And **RSV is the commonest single virus.**

---

### Bacteria change with age — this is exam gold

| Age | Bugs |
|---|---|
| **Neonate** | *E. coli*, *Klebsiella*, **Group B strep**, *Staph aureus* |
| **3 months – 3 years** | ***Strep pneumoniae* (> 70% of bacterial causes)**, *H. influenzae* |
| **> 3 years** | *Strep pneumoniae*, *Strep pyogenes*, *Bordetella pertussis*, *Staph aureus* |

**Why does the neonatal list look so different?**

> Because the newborn picks up organisms **from the birth canal**, not from the air. Group B strep and *E. coli* live there.

---

### Atypicals also follow age

- **Neonate** → *Chlamydia trachomatis*
- **3 months – 3 years** → *Chlamydia pneumoniae*
- **> 3 years** → *Mycoplasma pneumoniae*
- **HIV / immunocompromised** → *Pneumocystis jiroveci*

---

### One humbling fact

> In **20–60%** of pneumonias, **no pathogen is ever identified.**

So most treatment is **empiric — guided by age**. That is exactly why the age table matters so much.

---

## How the lung defends itself — and how germs beat it

The lower airway is normally **sterile**. Three physical defences keep it that way:

- **Mucociliary clearance** — the escalator
- **Secretory IgA** — the antibody coating
- **Coughing** — the emergency ejection

Plus immune defences: **alveolar macrophages**, **secretory IgA**, **other immunoglobulins**.

---

### Four ways germs get in

- **Inhalation** of droplets
- **Descending** from a URTI or another LRTI
- **Aspiration**
- **Haematogenous** spread (through the blood)

Then: organisms settle in **small bronchioles and alveoli**, multiply, and spread outwards.

---

### The final common pathway — say this in one line

**Pathogen multiplies** → immune cells (macrophages, neutrophils, lymphocytes, eosinophils) engulf and **release cytokines** → **apoptosis + leaky vessels + oedema** → **cell destruction and alveolar congestion** →

> **Oxygen cannot cross the alveolar membrane.**

**That single sentence is why the child is breathless, tachypnoeic and cyanosed.**

---

### How bad it gets depends on 3 things

- **Health of the individual**
- **The organism**
- **The size of the inoculum** (how many germs got in)

---

## The four pathological stages — follow the sputum

This is the easiest way to remember them.

| Stage | Day | What is in the alveoli | Sputum |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Vascular congestion** | **within 24 h** | oedema fluid, **many bacteria, few neutrophils** | **CLEAR** |
| **2. Red hepatization** | **day 2–4** | **RBCs**, neutrophils, epithelial cells, fibrin | **RUSTY** |
| **3. Grey hepatization** | **day 4–6** | fibrinopurulent exudate, haemosiderin | — |
| **4. Resolution** | **day 8–9** | enzymes break down the exudate | — |

---

### Why "hepatization"?

Because the lung becomes **as solid as liver** (*hepar* = liver).

**Red** first — because **red cells** flood in.

**Grey** next — because those red cells are **haemolysed**, and the **haemosiderin** left behind turns it **grey-brown to yellow**.

> **Red → the RBCs arrive. Grey → the RBCs have broken down.**

---

### Two details worth marks

- In **stage 1** there are **many bacteria but FEW neutrophils** — the germs arrived before the defence did
- By **stage 3** the lung is **firm but NO LONGER CONGESTED** — the blood has drained away

---

## Investigations — start with WHY, not WHAT

Six goals:

1. **Confirm** the diagnosis
2. Determine the **severity**
3. Establish **complications**
4. Exclude **differentials**
5. Determine the **causative organism**
6. **Monitor response** to treatment

> A list of tests without goals is unmemorable. Attach each test to a goal and it sticks.

---

### Chest X-ray — the workhorse

**Two things it does:** confirms the diagnosis, **and** shows complications (effusion, empyema).

**Two patterns to know cold:**

> **Viral** → **hyperinflation + bilateral interstitial infiltrates + peribronchial cuffing**
>
> **Pneumococcal** → **confluent LOBAR consolidation**

**Five findings to list:**

- Peribronchial infiltrates
- Lobar/segmental consolidation
- **Air bronchograms**
- Airspace obliteration
- Hilar lymphadenopathy

---

### Air bronchogram — understand it, do not memorise it

Normally an **air-filled bronchus** sits inside **air-filled lung**. Air against air — **you see nothing**.

Now fill the **alveoli** with pus, but leave the **bronchus** open.

> Now a **black branching tube** appears against a **white background**.

**That is an air bronchogram, and it proves the disease is in the alveoli — not the airway.**

---

### Microbiology — the honest truth

> **Blood culture identifies the organism in only 20–30%.**

And:

- **Sputum m/c/s is unreliable in children** — contaminated by upper airway flora
- **Nasopharyngeal and throat swabs are unreliable** — they grow normal commensals
- **Viral tissue culture takes 5–10 days** — too slow to help today's patient

**Definitive bacterial diagnosis needs isolation from blood, pleural fluid or lung.**

---

### The clever test

**Countercurrent immuno-electrophoresis of urine** detects **antigens of capsulated bacteria** (*S. pneumoniae*, *H. influenzae*) —

> **even AFTER antibiotics have been started.**

Culture goes negative once antibiotics are given. **Antigen does not.** That is the whole point of the test.

---

### White cell count — a rough guide, not proof

| | Count | Cells |
|---|---|---|
| **Viral** | usually **NOT above 20,000** | **lymphocytes** |
| **Bacterial** | **15,000–40,000** | **granulocytes** |

> The ranges **overlap** between 15,000 and 20,000. Never diagnose on the WBC alone.

---

## Differentials — sort them by what mimics what

**Airway noise instead of lung disease:** bronchiolitis, asthma, bronchitis

**Chronic lung suppuration:** bronchiectasis, lung abscess

**Fluid, not pus:** pulmonary oedema

**Blocked, not infected:** **atelectasis from a foreign body or thick mucus**

**No germ at all:** chemical pneumonitis

> **Never forget the foreign body.** A sudden onset in a well toddler with unilateral signs is inhalation until proven otherwise.

---

## Complications — SHARE 4Ps

**Acute:**

- **S**epticaemia
- **H**eart failure
- **A**telectasis
- **R**espiratory failure
- **E**mpyema
- **4 Ps** — **P**leural effusion, **P**yo-pneumothorax, **P**neumothorax, **P**neumatocele

*(plus subcutaneous emphysema — rare, except in measles)*

**Chronic:**

- **Lung abscess**
- **Bronchiectasis**

---

## Treatment — three principles, and everything hangs on them

1. **Eradicate the infection** → antibiotics, antivirals, antifungals
2. **Give supportive treatment** → oxygen, fluids, antipyretics, feeding, nursing, physiotherapy, bronchodilator trial
3. **Manage the complications** → **digitalize** in CCF · **drain** pleural fluid · **transfuse** if anaemic

---

### How do you choose the antibiotic?

Five guides:

- **Age** of the patient
- **Immune status**
- **Local epidemiology**
- **Radiologic findings**
- **Microbiology results** — *if available*

> Note the last three words. **Usually they are not available.** So **age** does most of the work.

---

### When to admit — group them

**Too young:** **under 6 months**

**Too sick:** toxic appearance · severe respiratory distress · **needs oxygen** · dehydration · vomiting

**Bad background:** **sickle cell with acute chest syndrome** · immunocompromised · **multiple lobe involvement**

**Treatment is failing or won't happen:** no response to oral antibiotics · **non-compliant parents**

---

## Prevention — three levels

**Immunization** — measles, diphtheria, pertussis, *H. influenzae*

**Health education** — less overcrowding · adequate nutrition · **breastfeeding** · **no kissing children when you have an RTI** · less cigarette and wood smoke

**Legislation** — government laws on pollution and smoking

---

## Putting it all together

Pneumonia is **germ-caused inflammation of the gas-exchange tissue**. Chemicals cause **pneumonitis** instead.

There are **two shapes**: **lobar** (one solid block — dull, increased fremitus, bronchial breathing, **pleuritic pain**) and **bronchopneumonia** (scattered patches — resonant, crepitations, **chest pain unusual**). In both, the **trachea stays central**.

**Viruses are commonest overall** (RSV top), but **bacteria change with age** — and since **no organism is found in 20–60%**, that age table is what actually guides your antibiotic.

The pathology runs **congestion → red → grey → resolution**, and the **sputum goes clear then rusty**.

The whole clinical picture comes from one sentence: **inflammation blocks oxygen crossing the alveolar membrane.**
