# Fungal Skin Infections

*Dr Odochi Ewurum, MBBS, FWACP, FMCPaed — Paediatrics, 24/06/26*

## Introduction

Spores of fungi occur in the **hot and humid or arid tropical environment**.

**Poor personal and community hygiene, closeness among large families, and institutions such as schools, camps and barracks** favour the spread of fungal infections among children.

Most of these fungal infections affect the **skin, hair, nails and mucosa**.

## Classification of fungal skin infections

### Superficial

- Candidiasis
- Tinea capitis
- Tinea corporis
- Tinea pedis
- Pityriasis versicolor

### Deep

- Mycetoma (Madura foot)
- Blastomycosis
- Coccidiomycosis
- Histoplasmosis
- Sporotrichosis

## Candidiasis

An infection caused by ***Candida albicans, C. tropicalis, C. stellatoidea, C. krusei***, or other yeasts of the genus *Candida*.

The infections usually occur on the **skin, mucous membranes, nail folds and nails, gastrointestinal tract and genital tract**. In systemic candida infections, the **viscera** may be involved.

The **skin infection** may present as **dusky red macerated or erosive lesions** in the skin folds of the **neck, flanks, large flexures and limbs**. Fissuring and bleeding may follow the scratching of affected areas, with occurrence of **secondary bacterial infection**.

### Oral candidiasis (thrush)

Caused by yeast-like fungi of the genus *Candida* — most commonly ***Candida albicans***.

Characterised by **curdy or whitish-grey, friable, cheesy pseudomembranous patches or plaques on markedly reddened mucosa**.

The plaques of thrush **invade the mucosa superficially** and may be found on the **lips, buccal mucosa, tongue, and palate**, extending to the **oesophagus/pharynx**.

> **Removal of plaques from these surfaces may cause mild punctate areas of bleeding, which helps to confirm the diagnosis.**

**Predisposing factors to candidiasis:**

1. Diabetes mellitus
2. Retroviral disease
3. Prolonged antibiotic and corticosteroid use
4. Leukaemia, etc.

**Treatment:**

- Antifungal drugs such as **clotrimazole, nystatin, amphotericin B, miconazole gel**
- Proper oral hygiene

## Tinea capitis

A **dermatophyte infection of the scalp**, most often caused by ***Trichophyton tonsurans***, occasionally by ***Microsporum canis***, and much less commonly by other *Microsporum* and *Trichophyton* species.

Lesions occur as **erythematous and scaly circular plaques (ringworm)**, within which the infected hairs become **brittle and broken with different lengths**.

Multiple affected sites produce a **moth-eaten appearance** of hair.

Scaling and inflammation of the scalp are variable. Numerous **confluent patches of alopecia** develop, and patients may complain of **severe pruritus**.

### Kerion

Sometimes the host's **cellular immune response** to tinea capitis may lead to the development of an **inflammatory and infected boggy mass covered in crusts, pus-filled bumps and localized hair loss**, referred to as a **kerion**.

### Favus

A **severe, chronic form** of tinea capitis, typically caused by the dermatophyte ***Trichophyton schoenleinii***.

Characterised by **thick, yellowish, cup-shaped yellow crusts called scutula** that develop around hair follicles, often accompanied by a **distinctive mousy odour**.

Also termed **honey-combed scalp lesions** with yellow crusts and scales.

> If left untreated, it **destroys the hair follicles**, causes **matting of the hair** and **permanent scarring hair loss (alopecia)**.

### Diagnosis and treatment

**Diagnosis:** Scraping and **KOH examination**.

**Treatment:**

- Oral **griseofulvin, itraconazole or terbinafine**
- Topical antifungal cream, shampoo

## Tinea corporis

A fungal infection of the **glabrous skin**, excluding the palms, soles and groin.

**Most common aetiologic organisms:**

- *Trichophyton rubrum*
- *Trichophyton mentagrophytes*
- Infections with *Microsporum canis* are also frequent

**Characteristic appearance:** a **dry, mildly erythematous, elevated, scaly papule or plaque** that **spreads centrifugally and clears centrally** to form the characteristic **annular lesion**. The **active edge shows vesicles and pustules**.

It is usually acquired by **direct contact from an infected person, surfaces, and pets** (*M. canis*).

### Other forms by site

1. **Tinea faciale** — infection of the glabrous skin localized to the face
2. **Tinea axillaris** — infection of the axilla
3. **Tinea cruris** — infection of the groin
4. **Tinea manuum** — infection of the hands
5. **Tinea unguium** — infection of the nails

### Diagnosis and treatment

**Diagnosis:** clinical; skin scraping and **KOH examination**; **culture of skin scrapings** for identifying the species of dermatophyte.

**Treatment:**

1. **Topical antifungal agents** — e.g. imidazoles, terbinafine, naftifine
2. In severe cases, **oral griseofulvin, itraconazole**

## Tinea pedis (athlete's foot)

A fungal infection of the **toe webs and soles of the feet**.

**Usual etiologic agents:**

1. *Trichophyton rubrum*
2. *Trichophyton mentagrophytes*
3. *Epidermophyton floccosum*

**Features:**

- The **subdigital crevices are fissured**, with **maceration and peeling** of the surrounding skin
- Less commonly, a **chronic diffuse hyperkeratosis of the sole** occurs, with only mild erythema
- Occasionally, the papules progress to **vesicles and bullae** that may become pustular
- **Severe tenderness, itching, and a persistent foul odour** are characteristic

### Diagnosis and treatment

**Diagnosis:** fungal mycelia seen on microscopic examination of a **KOH preparation**, or by culture.

**Treatment:**

1. **Avoidance of occlusive footwear**; careful **drying between the toes** after bathing
2. Topical **zinc undecylenate powder**, **imidazole cream**, oral **griseofulvin**

## Pityriasis versicolor

A common **chronic fungal infection of the stratum corneum**.

Caused by the **dimorphic yeast _Malassezia furfur_**, found in areas of skin that are **rich in sebum production**.

The disease is most prevalent in **adolescents and young adults**.

**Predisposing factors:**

- A warm humid environment
- Excessive sweating
- Occlusion
- High plasma cortisol levels
- Immunosuppression
- Malnourishment
- Genetically determined susceptibility

**Lesions:** either **hypopigmented or hyperpigmented macules covered with a fine scale**, which often **merge to form confluent patches**.

**Sites:** most commonly the **neck, upper chest, back, and upper arms**.

**Diagnosis:** **Wood lamp**, KOH preparation of scrapings, skin biopsy.

**Treatment:**

- **Selenium sulfide suspension** applied overnight
- **Imidazole or terbinafine cream** twice daily for 2–4 weeks
- Oral therapy with **ketoconazole, fluconazole, or itraconazole**

## Histoplasmosis

A **chronic infection** caused by ***Histoplasma capsulatum*** and ***H. duboisii***, dimorphic fungi acquired by **inhalation of fungal spores**.

These are found in the **soil and in the droppings of some animals** e.g. **bats, poultry**.

The **primary infection is in the lungs**, with **haematogenous spread** to the **liver, bone marrow, spleen, lymph nodes and subcutaneous tissue**.

Skin lesions appear as **papules, pustules and nodules**, which may be found throughout the body and are caused by the fungus spreading to infect the skin.

**_H. duboisii_ (African histoplasmosis)** presents as **subcutaneous nodules which tend to umbilicate and suppurate or ulcerate** as they grow.

**_H. capsulatum_** characteristically affects the **lungs**, and may present with **productive cough and constitutional symptoms**.

**Diagnosis:** isolation and identification of the causative fungus in cultures from **sputum, bone marrow, lymph node biopsy, or pus or scrapings from ulcers**.

**Treatment:** IV **amphotericin B**; oral **itraconazole or ketoconazole**.

## Sporotrichosis

A chronic infection which may follow **accidental implantation into the skin of _Sporothrix schenckii_**, which **grows on thorny plants**.

The infection typically begins as a **firm, painless or mildly tender subcutaneous nodule (bump) at the site of skin trauma**, and presents as a **purple, violaceous, hyperpigmented brown, or dark grey nodule**.

The primary nodule **grows larger, becomes pustular, and may open into a chronic, hollowed-out sore (ulceration)**.

Early lesions are **indurated subcutaneous nodules at the sites of implantation**.

Infection may spread by **lymphatic or haematogenous routes** to muscles, bones, joints or other tissues. **Inhalation of spores may cause lung infection.**

**Diagnosis:** microscopic demonstration of the fungus in preparations from scrapings or biopsy.

**Treatment:** **potassium iodide**, IV **amphotericin B**, oral **itraconazole**.

## Blastomycosis

A chronic infection caused by ***Blastomyces dermatitidis***, acquired by **inhalation of fungal spores**.

The **primary infection is in the lungs**, with **haematogenous spread to the skin, bone and genital tract**.

Presents as **irregularly crusted plaques, superficial ulcerative lesions, or firm subcutaneous nodules on exposed parts of the body**.

**Diagnosis:** characteristic **budding cells** seen in KOH preparation of materials from the lesion.

**Treatment:** IV **amphotericin B**.

## Coccidiomycosis

Caused by ***Coccidioides immitis***, a dimorphic fungus acquired by **inhalation of fungal spores**.

The **primary infection is in the lungs**, with **haematogenous spread to the skin, subcutaneous tissue and the bones**.

Skin lesions consist of **panniculitis, abscesses and draining sinuses**.

**Treatment:** oral **ketoconazole or itraconazole**.

## Mycetoma (Madura foot)

A **chronic infection of the skin, subcutaneous tissue and bones of the feet**.

It takes a **very long time to develop** and is **rare in children**.

The microorganisms are typically **introduced into the body through minor injuries like a splinter or thorn prick**, which is why it is **common among agricultural workers**.

It can be caused by **both fungi and specific types of bacteria**:

- **Eumycetoma** — when the infection is caused by **fungi**
- **Actinomycetoma** — when caused by **filamentous bacteria**

**Aetiology:**

- **Actinomycetes** such as *Nocardia brasiliensis*, *Actinomadura madurae*, *Streptomyces somaliensis*
- **Fungi** such as *Pseudallescheria boydii*, *Madurella grisea*, or *Madurella mycetomatis*

**Treatment:** **itraconazole**.
