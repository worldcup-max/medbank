# Introduction to Pulmonology — the simple version

## One big idea holds this whole lecture together

> The airway is **one continuous road** — from the nose all the way down to the alveoli — and it is **built as one outgrowth from the gut tube** in the embryo.

Two consequences flow from that single idea, and they run through everything:

1. **Because it grows from the gut**, a wall (septum) has to separate the airway from the food pipe — and if that wall fails, you get **TOF** (a hole between them).
2. **Because it's one road**, an infection at the top (runny nose) can travel all the way down (pneumonia). No stop signs.

Hold those two, and the lecture organises itself.

---

## Part 1 — How the lung is built (embryology)

### The split

Early on there's one tube: the **foregut**.

A wall grows down the middle and splits it into two:

- **Front** → **trachea** (airway)
- **Back** → **oesophagus** (food pipe)

That wall is the **oesophagotracheal septum**, and it does its job in **week 4**.

> **If that wall fails → tracheo-oesophageal fistula (TOF)** = a connection between airway and food pipe = **surgical emergency in a newborn.**

The two tubes stay joined at **one** spot — the **larynx**.

---

### Who makes what (two tissue layers)

| Part of the airway | Comes from |
|---|---|
| **Lining** (larynx → alveoli) | **ENDOderm** (think: *inner* lining) |
| **Cartilage + muscle** (the wrapping) | **MESOderm** (think: *middle* structural stuff) |

> **Lining = endoderm. Wrapping = mesoderm.** Two layers, two jobs.

---

### Why the right lung is bigger — settled in the embryo

- **Left bud** → **2** bronchi, **2** lobes
- **Right bud** → **3** bronchi, **3** lobes

> The right lung is bigger **from the very beginning**. It's not random.

---

## Part 2 — Surfactant (the make-or-break for premature babies)

Two phases to know:

- **Pseudoglandular (5–17 wk)** — looks like a gland, **no gas exchange yet** ("false gland")
- **Canalicular (15–25 wk)** — airways widen; **type II cells start making surfactant**

### What surfactant does — in one sentence

> Surfactant **lowers surface tension** so the alveoli **don't collapse** when you breathe out.

Picture a wet balloon: the wet walls want to stick together and collapse. Surfactant is the slippery coat that stops that.

> **No surfactant → alveoli collapse every breath → RDS (respiratory distress syndrome).** This is exactly why **premature babies** struggle to breathe.

### One more fact people forget

> After birth, lungs grow by **making MORE alveoli**, not bigger ones. **Multiplication, not enlargement.**

---

## Part 3 — The road (anatomy of the tract)

### Say the road out loud, top to bottom

> **Nose → Pharynx → Epiglottis → Larynx → Trachea → Bronchi → Bronchioles → Alveolar sacs**

### The one dividing line

> The **larynx** splits the road:
> - **Above the larynx = UPPER** tract
> - **Below the larynx = LOWER** tract

| Upper (nose, sinuses, pharynx) | Lower (trachea, bronchi, bronchioles, alveoli) |
|---|---|
| Easy to examine (light) | Deep — need instruments |
| Milder illness | Serious disease lives here (pneumonia, asthma, bronchiolitis) |

---

### The trachea — three numbers

- **Starts:** cricoid cartilage at **C6**
- **Ends (splits):** sternal **angle of Louis** at **T4/T5**
- **Lined by:** ciliated columnar epithelium + goblet cells → **cilia sweep, mucus traps** = first-line defence

---

### The bronchi — the most tested fact in the lecture

| | Right bronchus | Left bronchus |
|---|---|---|
| Width | **wider** | narrower |
| Angle | **25° (more vertical)** | 45° (steeper) |

> **Foreign bodies go RIGHT.** Because the right bronchus is wider and straighter, an inhaled object drops into the **right lung (middle/lower lobes)**.
>
> **A child inhaled something? Look right first.**

---

### The lobes

- **Right lung = 3 lobes** (2 fissures: oblique + horizontal)
- **Left lung = 2 lobes** (1 fissure: oblique) — smaller because **the heart takes up room on the left**

> **Right = 3, Left = 2.** The heart is on the left, so the left lung gives up a lobe.

---

## Part 4 — Why anatomy becomes disease

> Because it's **one connected road**, infection **spreads DOWNWARD**.

Runny nose → bronchitis → bronchiolitis → pneumonia. Same germ, same road, just travelling further.

**That is the whole reason a "simple cold" in a child can become pneumonia in days.**

---

## Part 5 — Meeting the child (history first, always)

### The order is deliberate

> **History → Examination → Investigation.**
>
> History first, because it **tells you what to look for** and **which tests to order.** Skip it and everything downstream is unfocused and expensive.

### The history skeleton — 5 parts

**Bio-data · Presenting symptoms · Past medical history · Family history · Social history**

---

### Why AGE matters so much

Younger = more fragile, for real anatomical reasons:

- **Smaller airways** → a little swelling blocks a lot (resistance shoots up as the tube narrows)
- **Floppy chest wall** → it caves in (recessions) instead of pushing air → tires fast
- **Poor hypoxic reserve** → can go **apnoeic** quickly
- **Low reserve + high demand** → **crashes faster**

> A 2-month-old and an 8-year-old with the *same* bronchiolitis are **not equally safe** — the baby has far less reserve.

**Age also predicts the diagnosis:**

- **Neonate** → congenital, neonatal pneumonia, aspiration
- **Infant** → **bronchiolitis, pertussis**
- **Toddler** → viral wheeze, **foreign body**, croup
- **School-age** → **asthma, pneumonia**
- **Adolescent** → asthma, atypical pneumonia

---

### The big three symptoms — always characterise them

**Cough** — how long, what it sounds like (barking? whooping? productive?), when (night/after feeds), how bad, any sputum.

**Breathlessness** — how bad, how long, constant or episodic, rest or exertion.

**Chest pain** — is it **breathing-related (pleuritic = worse on inspiration/cough)** or **cardiac**?

---

### Noisy breathing — 6 words to know

> **Wheeze · Stridor · Snuffles · Rattles · Snoring · Grunting**

---

### The pattern that hands you the diagnosis

> **Recurrent wheeze + responds to bronchodilators + worse in harmattan = ASTHMA.**

Reversible + trigger-driven = asthma, before you've even examined.

---

### Red flags you must not miss

> **Weight loss + neck swelling + night sweats → think TB or lymphoma.**

---

### Family + social history — the hidden causes

- **Atopy in the family** (asthma/eczema/hay fever) → raises asthma risk
- **Chronic cough in family** → think **cystic fibrosis, ciliary dyskinesia**
- **Passive smoking** → damages mucosa, paralyses cilia, invites infection
- **Overcrowding** → spreads **TB and pneumonia**

---

## Putting it all together

The airway is **one road built from the gut tube**.

**From the gut:** a wall (septum) separates airway from food pipe — fail it and you get **TOF**. The **lining is endoderm, the wrapping is mesoderm**, and **right = 3 lobes, left = 2**.

**Surfactant** keeps alveoli open; without it (prematurity) you get **RDS**. Lungs grow by **more alveoli, not bigger ones**.

**One road** means infection **spreads down** — cold to pneumonia.

And when you meet the child: **history first**, let **age** shape your suspicion, characterise **cough, breathlessness, chest pain**, and catch the two classic patterns — **asthma** (recurrent, reversible, harmattan) and the **TB/lymphoma red flags** (weight loss, neck swelling, night sweats).
