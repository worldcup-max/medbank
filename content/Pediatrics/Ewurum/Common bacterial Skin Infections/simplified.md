# Common Bacterial Skin Infections — Simplified

**One line:** healthy skin fights bacteria off by itself; these six infections happen when that defence is breached.

---

## Why healthy skin doesn't get infected — 8 defences

**Physical / chemical (the first four):**

- **Dry** — bacteria need moisture
- **pH 5.5** — too acidic for most pathogens
- **Unsaturated long chain fatty acids** in skin lipids
- **Antibacterial substances in sebum**

**Immune (the next three):**

- Circulating **immunoglobulins**
- **Cellular immunity**
- **Delayed hypersensitivity**

**Biological (the eighth):**

- **Bacterial interference** — the harmless bacteria already living there crowd out the dangerous ones

---

## What breaks those defences — 9 factors

**Break the surface:** trauma (abrasions, scratches, cuts) · insect bites

**Change the environment:** increased humidity

**Weaken the host:** malnutrition · severe illness · natural or acquired immune depression · iatrogenic immune suppression

**Other:** poor personal or community hygiene · coexisting skin disorders like atopic dermatitis

---

## The six infections — by depth

> This is the key to the whole topic. **They are the same organism going progressively deeper.**

| Depth | Condition |
|---|---|
| **Epidermis** | Impetigo |
| **One hair follicle, superficial** | Folliculitis |
| **One follicle, into subcutaneous tissue** | Furuncle (boil) |
| **A group of follicles** | Carbuncle |
| **Deep layers and underlying tissue** | Cellulitis |
| **Whole skin surface (toxin-mediated)** | SSSS |

---

## Folliculitis

**One hair follicle, superficial.**

Small, tender, red or pus-filled bumps around follicles — each with **a hair sticking out of the centre**.

Causes: bacterial, fungal, viral, parasitic.

Sites: face, scalp, thighs, buttocks.

**Treat:** warm compresses · antibacterial wash (benzoyl peroxide) · topical **mupirocin** or **clindamycin** · oral **cephalexin/doxycycline** if severe · stop shaving and picking.

---

## Furunculosis (boils)

**Deeper — into subcutaneous tissue.** Usually ***S. aureus***.

Either follows folliculitis, or starts as a deep tender nodule.

**The progression to remember:**

> Indurated lesion → central necrosis → suppuration → ruptures and discharges a **central core** of necrotic tissue → follicle destroyed → **heals with a scar**

**Sites:** scalp, face, back of neck, axillae, buttocks, groin.

**Predisposing:** obesity · hyperhidrosis · maceration · friction · pre-existing dermatitis

**Treat:** antibiotics · hygiene · hot moist compress · **incision and drainage**

---

## Carbuncles

**A group of contiguous follicles**, with **multiple drainage points**. Caused by ***S. aureus***.

Painful, tender, firm-to-hard, indurated lumps.

> Big enough to make the child systemically ill: **fever, leukocytosis, bacteraemia**.

**Sites:** back of neck, shoulder, hip, thigh.

**Treat:** **cloxacillin, cephalexin** · if penicillin-allergic: cephalosporin, clindamycin, erythromycin · if **MRSA**: **rifampicin, cotrimoxazole**

---

## Impetigo

**Epidermis only. Contagious.** Spread by direct contact or fomites.

Children **4–7 years**. Face — around nose and mouth — and hands.

**The signature:** blisters rupture → yellow exudate dries → **honey-coloured crust**.

### Two types

| | **Bullous** | **Non-bullous** |
|---|---|---|
| Organism | **_S. aureus_ phage group 2** — always | *S. aureus* or *S. pyogenes*, or mixed |
| Skin | **Intact** skin | **Trauma** sites |
| Lesion | Flaccid transparent **bullae** | 1–3 cm honey-crusted lesions |
| Age | **Neonates, under 5s** | Any |
| Frequency | Less common | **More common** |
| Systemic | **Common** | Lymphadenopathy |

**Predisposing lesions:** insect bites, abrasions, lacerations, chickenpox, scabies, pediculosis, burns.

Spread around the body by **fingers, clothing and towels**.

**Treat:** saline or aluminium acetate → then **2% mupirocin** · saline or **potassium permanganate soaks** to lift crusts · **fusidic acid** as alternative · systemic symptoms → **cloxacillin** or beta-lactamase resistant e.g. cephalexin · **MRSA → clindamycin**

---

## Cellulitis

**Deeper layers and underlying tissue.** ***Staph*** or ***Strep*** through a break in the skin.

**Painful, red, swollen, warm — and spreads quickly.**

**Risk factors:** skin injuries (cuts, surgical wounds, insect bites, tattoos, piercings) · eczema, athlete's foot, psoriasis · diabetes, HIV, immunosuppressants · lymphoedema, obesity

**Features:** redness and swelling · pain/tenderness · warmth · blisters, fever, swollen nodes if severe

**Treat:** oral & IV antibiotics covering strep and staph · wound care and drainage · **elevate above heart level** and rest · analgesia

**Complications — this is where it gets serious:**

> Bacteraemia · Endocarditis · Osteomyelitis · Suppurative arthritis · Thrombophlebitis

---

## Staphylococcal scalded skin syndrome (Ritter's disease)

**Not local infection — a TOXIN disease.**

Caused by **exfoliative toxins A and B** from *S. aureus* **phage group B**.

The toxin is made at one site and travels in the blood to strip the whole skin.

**What you see:**

- **Scarlatiniform erythema**, diffuse — favouring **flexural and periorificial** areas
- Extensive superficial **sterile flaccid bullae** → deroof → **raw red moist skin like a hot water burn**
- **Circumpolar erythema, radial crusting and fissuring around eyes, mouth, nose**
- Child appears **toxic**

**Before the rash:** fever, rhinitis, conjunctivitis, irritability, malaise, poor feeding, **exquisite tenderness**.

> **Nikolsky's sign positive** — the epidermis separates when you apply shearing pressure.

**Treat:** **flucloxacillin, erythromycin** · analgesics · **emollient**, clean with **isotonic saline** · **correct fluid and electrolyte balance** (the skin is a burn — fluid is lost through it)
