# Bronchiolitis — the simple version

## One sentence to anchor everything

> Bronchiolitis = a **virus** blocks the **tiniest airways** of a **baby**, and because those airways are already tiny, even a little swelling causes a big problem.

Three words carry the whole topic: **virus · small airways · baby.** Hold them and the rest follows.

---

## Who gets it, and why babies

> **First 2 years of life. Peak 2–8 months (average 6 months). Boys 2:1.**

**Why babies specifically?** Their **peripheral airways are innately tiny.**

Here's the key physics:

> **Airway resistance rises FOUR-FOLD when the radius halves.**

So in a baby's already-narrow bronchiole, a small amount of swelling + mucus makes resistance shoot up. The same infection in an adult barely narrows their big airways.

> **This "small airway" fact is the single most important idea in the lecture** — it explains why it's a disease of infants and why they struggle so much.

---

## What causes it

> **Viruses — RSV is the commonest.** (Also adeno, parainfluenza, metapneumovirus, rhino, flu.)

Bacteria cause only a few cases. So think **virus first.**

**Two nasty ones to remember:**
- **RSV group A** → more severe than group B
- **Adenovirus (7, 11, 21)** → severe, can cause **bronchiolitis obliterans** (permanent scarring)

---

## Predisposing factors — group them

**Cold air:** rainy season, harmattan
**No protection:** not breastfed
**Lots of germs around:** overcrowding, day-care, older siblings, smoke exposure
**Weak baby:** low birth weight, chronic lung disease, heart lesions

> Notice how many overlap with pneumonia's risk factors — same story: cold air, no breastfeeding, crowding, smoke.

---

## How the airway gets blocked (pathophysiology)

Three things narrow the bronchiole:

> **1. Swelling (oedema) + 2. Mucus & debris + 3. Muscle squeeze** → smaller lumen → high resistance.

Now the clever part — **the ball-valve:**

> The airway is **narrower on breathing OUT** than breathing in.

So air can get **IN** past the block but struggles to get **OUT** → air gets **trapped** → the lung **hyperinflates.**

- **Partial block (ball-valve)** → air trapping → **hyperinflation** (this is why the liver and spleen feel enlarged — the lungs are pushing them down!)
- **Complete block (check-valve)** → trapped air gets absorbed → the segment **collapses (atelectasis)**

**Then oxygen falls:**
- Early → **hypoxaemia** (V/Q mismatch)
- Late, when the baby **tires** → **CO₂ rises (hypercapnia)** — a danger sign

---

## What you see (clinical features)

**It starts like a cold** — sneezing, clear runny nose — then goes down into the chest:

> **Wheezy cough + fast breathing + working hard to breathe.**

**The signs of a struggling baby:**
- **Nasal flaring, retractions** (increased work of breathing)
- **Fine inspiratory crepitations + rhonchi + wheeze**
- **Prolonged expiration** (they can't push air out)
- **Reduced breath sounds** (badly blocked areas)

**Two danger signs in the youngest babies (<2 months):**

> **APNOEA** (stops breathing) and **bradycardia** — these can come with little warning. Watch small babies closely.

**The trick fact:**

> **Hepatomegaly + splenomegaly** here is NOT true organ enlargement — the **hyperinflated lungs push the diaphragm down**, shoving the liver and spleen lower so you feel them.

---

## Investigations — mostly to confirm, not to diagnose

The diagnosis is **clinical**. Tests support it:

**Chest X-ray** — often normal, or shows **HYPERINFLATION**:
> **Splayed ribs, flat diaphragms, relative microcardia** (small-looking heart because the lungs are so inflated), patchy atelectasis.

**FBC** — usually **normal WBC** (it's viral, not bacterial).

**Pulse oximetry** — the practical one: detects hypoxaemia, decides oxygen need.

**Viral testing** — only if unsure or for epidemiology.

---

## Differentials — what else wheezes?

The big one to always consider:

> **Asthma** — but asthma is recurrent and responds to bronchodilators; a first wheeze in a 6-month-old in the rainy season is bronchiolitis.

Also: WARI, heart failure, **pertussis**, **foreign body**, reflux, croup, epiglottitis.

---

## Treatment — the honest truth

> **It's mostly SUPPORTIVE. There is no magic cure — you keep the baby oxygenated and fed while the virus runs its course.**

The essentials:
- **Oxygen** (the one that matters most)
- **Fluids and feeds**
- **Trial** of bronchodilators (try; stop if no help)
- Steroids only **if benefit outweighs risk**

The special ones:
- **Ribavirin** (antiviral) — only for **severe cases, immunocompromised, or cyanotic heart disease**; needs a special SPAG aerosol device
- **Antibiotics** — only if there's a real bacterial infection on top

> **Do not reach for antibiotics reflexively** — it's a virus.

---

## Prevention

- **Palivizumab** (a monoclonal antibody injection) — immunoprophylaxis for high-risk babies
- **Hand-washing** (the biggest practical lever — it spreads on hands)
- **Breastfeeding**
- Keep sick children out of creches; **avoid smoke**

---

## Putting it all together

Bronchiolitis is a **viral (RSV) blockage of the tiny airways in a baby under 2** (peak ~6 months, boys 2:1).

It matters because **infant airways are tiny and resistance rises four-fold as they narrow** — so a little swelling + mucus causes a **ball-valve** that traps air and **hyperinflates** the lung (hence the pushed-down liver and spleen).

It starts as a **cold, becomes a wheezy struggling chest**, and the danger signs are **apnoea and bradycardia in the under-2-months** and **rising CO₂ when the baby tires**.

Diagnosis is **clinical**; treatment is **supportive — oxygen and feeds** — not antibiotics; and prevention is **hand-washing, breastfeeding, and palivizumab** for high-risk infants.
