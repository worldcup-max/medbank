## Introduction

**Bronchiolitis** is an **inflammatory obstruction of the small airways** — the **bronchioles and surrounding tissues**.

- It is an **acute infection of the lower respiratory tract**
- **Predominantly a viral disease**, with few cases attributed to other organisms

## Epidemiology

- Peak incidence during the **rainy season** in tropical countries (winter in temperate countries)
- Prevalence is **higher in urban areas**
- Occurs during the **first 2 years of life**
- **Peak age 2–8 months (average 6 months)**
- **Commoner in males — M:F 2:1**
- Most cases are **mild, lasting 7–10 days**
- **Low socio-economic status** increases the risk of hospitalization
- **Mortality** in hospitalized cases is about **1–2%**, occurring mostly in children **< 6 months**; higher in developing countries due to poor nutrition and healthcare

### Predisposing factors

1. Rainy season
2. Cold, dry, dusty harmattan season
3. Babies who are **not breastfed**
4. **Overcrowding**, day-care centres
5. Exposure to **cigarette smoke**
6. **Low birth weight**
7. **Chronic lung disease**
8. **Congenital heart lesions**
9. **Older family members** as a common source of infection

## Aetiology

**Viruses (predominant):**

- **Respiratory syncytial virus (RSV)** — the commonest
- Adenovirus
- Parainfluenza 1, 2, 3
- Human metapneumovirus
- Bocavirus
- Rhinovirus
- Influenza virus

**Bacteria (few cases):**

- *Mycoplasma pneumoniae*, *Chlamydia pneumoniae*, *Simkania negevensis*, *Staphylococcus aureus*, *Staph epidermidis*, *Klebsiella pneumoniae*

## Pathogenesis

**Acquisition** occurs through:

- **Inhalation** of infected respiratory droplets
- **Inoculation** of the nostrils/conjunctivae from contaminated hands of contacts

The initial viral infection and replication occurs in the **infant's upper respiratory tract**, then spreads down.

Several **risk factors** determine host vulnerability, infectivity and severity — **host, immunological, environmental** factors, and **microbiological characteristics** of the pathogen.

### Host-related factors

- **Age-related peripheral airway dimension — the dominant factor.** The innately **smaller peripheral airways** of infants have a greater propensity for **severe obstruction**
- **Paucity of avenues for collateral ventilation** (e.g. pores of Kohn, canals of Lambert)
- **Comparatively greater number of mucus-secreting (goblet) cells** in infants
- **Genetic age-related airway hyper-reactivity** to RSV and parainfluenza type 3

### Immunological factors

RSV incites complex immune responses:

- **Degranulation of eosinophils** and release of **eosinophil cationic protein** (cytotoxic to airway epithelium)
- Increased **Toll-like receptor-4** expression
- Increased lymphokines, chemokines, cytokines, **IgE antibody** and inflammatory mediators — **IL-8, MIP-1α, RANTES, monocyte chemotactic protein-1, neutrophil chemotactic factors, interferon-γ, leukotriene C4, virus-specific IgE, IgG-4, histamine and eosinophilic cationic protein**

### Microbiological factors

- **RSV group A** is associated with **greater severity** than group B
- **Parainfluenza type 3** is frequently associated with severity in blacks
- **Adenovirus types 7, 11, 21** are associated with severe bronchiolitis, more likely complicated by **bronchiolitis obliterans**

## Pathology

- **Necrosis of the respiratory epithelium** and destruction of ciliated cells
- **Intraluminal bronchiolar plugs** of sloughed cellular debris and fibrin
- **Peribronchial infiltration** by mononuclear cells
- Extensive epithelial destruction, **pulmonary parenchymal necrosis** and **hyaline membrane formation** — all **more in adenovirus** than RSV
- A **profligate cellular and humoral immune response** with cellular injury — in RSV

**Recovery:** epithelial regeneration begins about **4 days** after the acute phase; **ciliary activity** is restored in **~2 weeks or more**; sloughed plugs are removed by scavenging **macrophages**.

## Pathophysiology

**Oedema of the bronchiolar mucosa + mucus and cellular debris + muscle constriction** → **reduced lumen size** → **increased resistance** in the small airways during **both inspiration and expiration**.

Because resistance rises **exponentially (four-fold)** as airway radius falls, the **work of breathing, trapped (dead-space) air and respiratory rate all increase**.

- The airway radius is **smaller during expiration**, so obstruction causes **early air trapping** beyond the site and **progressive hyperinflation** — a **ball-valve** obstruction
- If obstruction becomes **complete (check-valve)** → **resorption of trapped distal air** → **patchy atelectasis**
- **Hypoxaemia** results from **ventilation–perfusion mismatch** early in the disease
- With severe obstruction and tiring effort, less air reaches the alveoli → **hypercapnia** (raised PCO₂) may develop

## Clinical features

**Symptoms:** sneezing and clear rhinorrhoea; diminished appetite; fever **38.5–39°C** (or subnormal temperature); respiratory distress; **paroxysmal wheezy cough**; dyspnoea; irritability; **tachypnoea**; **apnoea**.

**Signs:**

- **Markedly increased work of breathing** — nasal flaring, retractions
- **Fine inspiratory crepitations** and **rhonchi**
- **Prolonged expiratory phase**
- **Reduced breath sounds**
- **Hepatomegaly and splenomegaly** — due to **lung hyperinflation** pushing the organs down (not true organomegaly)

## Complications and co-morbidities

- **Dehydration, apnoea and bradycardia** — especially in infants **< 2 months**
- Acute otitis media
- **Hypoxaemia** ± hypercarbia
- Viral pneumonia; secondary bacterial pneumonia
- **Recurrent wheezing or asthma**
- Urinary tract infection

## Investigations

**Chest X-ray (PA and lateral):**

- May be **normal**
- **Peribronchial thickening**
- **Hyperinflation** — "splaying of the ribs", flattened diaphragmatic domes, **relative microcardia**
- **Patchy atelectasis**, increased bronchovascular markings, interstitial streaks from the hilum
- Increased **antero-posterior diameter** on lateral film

**FBC** — total WBC and differentials usually **normal**.

**Viral testing** (rapid immunofluorescence, PCR, or culture) — if diagnosis is uncertain or for epidemiology.

**Pulse oximetry** — detect hypoxaemia and guide oxygen need.

**Blood gas/acid-base** — for aberrations like metabolic acidosis.

## Differential diagnosis

Bronchial asthma; wheeze-associated respiratory infection (WARI); congestive cardiac failure; pertussis; aspirated foreign body; gastro-oesophageal reflux disease; croup; retropharyngeal abscess; acute epiglottitis; impacted foreign body in the upper airways.

## Treatment

**Usually supportive.** Current recommendations:

- **Oxygen therapy**
- **Increased fluids and feeds**
- **Trial of bronchodilators**
- **Corticosteroids / anti-inflammatory agents** (e.g. leukotriene) — only if benefit outweighs risk
- **Antiviral (ribavirin)** — delivered by a **small-particle aerosol generator (SPAG)** via an oxygen hood/ventilator, over 3–5 days (18–20 h/day); reserved for **severe cases, the immunocompromised, or infants with cyanotic CHD**
- **Antibiotics** — only with concomitant severe bacterial infection
- **Physiotherapy** — for secretion clearance; not routine
- **Antipyretics**, nasal decongestion, cough mixtures

## Prevention and control

- **Palivizumab immunoprophylaxis** (intramuscular)
- **Health education on hand decontamination** — health workers, parents, nursery/creche staff; attention to inter-child hand-washing
- **Encouraging breastfeeding**
- Minimizing spread in creches/nurseries by excusing children and staff with acute respiratory illness
- Discouraging **cigarette smoking and domestic smoke exposure**
