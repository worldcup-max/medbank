# Neonatal Investigations — the simple version

## Why so many tests on a tiny baby

> A newborn can't tell you what's wrong — so **investigations do the talking.** Small samples, screening several systems at once.

Group them so they aren't a scary list: **homeostasis** (glucose, gas, electrolytes, bone), **haematology** (FBC + film), **infection** (culture, CRP, LP), **organ function** (LFTs, thyroid).

---

## The blood count — three questions

> **Enough red cells?** Hb/Hct. **Why anaemic?** Reticulocytes + MCV. **Enough platelets?** Platelet count.

Two newborn traps:

> **Low MCV → iron deficiency, thalassaemia, ABO incompatibility.**
> Machines **over-count white cells** because newborns have **nucleated red cells** — a manual **blood film** fixes it.

---

## Transfusion thresholds — one logic each

**Red cells:**
> **Sicker + younger = transfuse at a HIGHER Hb.** A ventilated baby is kept higher (~12) than a stable baby (~7.5–10).

**Platelets:**
> **<25k** transfuse even if not bleeding · **<50k** if bleeding/surgery · **<100k** major bleed or neurosurgery.
> The higher the stakes, the higher the count you insist on.

---

## Spotting infection — the markers

> **Low ANC (<1500 low, <500 very low)** and a **high I:T ratio (>0.2)** both point to sepsis. **Very low WBC is scarier than high WBC.**

The two proteins:

> **CRP** is slow — **lags 12–24 h**, so a normal early CRP doesn't exclude sepsis. Use its **trend**.
> **Procalcitonin** is faster but useless in the **first 1–3 days.** Use it when the baby looks septic but CRP is normal.

The culture rule:

> **Positive culture / high CRP / neuro signs → do an LP** (check for meningitis). **One exception: CoNS sepsis** (often a contaminant).
> Rash + sepsis, or fever + encephalopathy → **herpes PCR + start aciclovir now.**

---

## Electrolytes — it's about WATER

> **Hyponatraemia** is usually **too much water** (SIADH) → treat with **fluid restriction**, not salt. Dangerous below 120.
> **Hypernatraemia** is **too little water** → correct **very slowly (≤0.5 meq/L/hr)** or you cause brain injury.

---

## Bone profile — the magnesium pearl

> Term calcium **bottoms out at 24 hours**; ~30% get early hypocalcaemia. **Calcium and phosphate move oppositely** (inverse).
> **Refractory low calcium won't correct until you fix the MAGNESIUM first.**

---

## Thyroid & liver

> **TSH is on the newborn screen** — catches congenital hypothyroidism (treatable, but disastrous if missed). Also check it in **prolonged jaundice** and **failure to thrive.**

Liver function splits three ways:

> **Synthetic** (albumin, clotting) · **cell injury** (AST/ALT, LDH) · **cholestasis** (GGT, ALP, direct bilirubin).
> A **raised direct bilirubin** is the alarm — work it up systematically (don't miss biliary atresia).

---

## Imaging — portable and low-radiation wins

> **Cranial ultrasound** (through the fontanelle) → **IVH**, hypoxic damage. **CXR** → RDS, meconium, pneumonia. **Echo** → heart defects, PDA. **Abdominal X-ray** → **NEC.**

---

## Putting it together

Investigations replace the history a newborn can't give. The **CBC** answers red-cell/platelet questions (watch the low MCV and the nucleated-RBC artefact). **Transfuse sicker babies at higher thresholds.** For **sepsis**, remember CRP is slow (use the trend), procalcitonin is fast but unreliable early, and a positive culture usually earns an **LP** (except CoNS). **Electrolytes are water problems** — SIADH = restrict fluid; hypernatraemia = correct slowly. On the **bone profile**, fix magnesium to fix calcium. **TSH** screens for hypothyroidism; a **raised direct bilirubin** demands a work-up; and **cranial ultrasound** is the newborn brain's friend.
