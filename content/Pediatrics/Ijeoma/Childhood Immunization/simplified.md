# Childhood Immunization — Simplified

**One line:** teach the body to fight a disease *before* it ever meets it.

---

## The 2 × 2 that organises the whole topic

**Ask two questions:**

1. **Does the body do the work, or is it given ready-made antibody?** → Active vs Passive
2. **Did it happen by itself, or did we do it?** → Natural vs Artificial

| | **NATURAL** | **ARTIFICIAL** |
|---|---|---|
| **PASSIVE** | From mother: **IgG via placenta** before birth; **IgA via breast milk** after | **Injected preformed antibodies** (human or animal serum) |
| **ACTIVE** | Having the infection — **clinical or subclinical** | **Vaccines or toxoids** |

> **Passive** = borrowed antibody, works **immediately**, wears off.
> **Active** = you make your own, takes **time**, lasts long.

---

## Passive immunization

**No challenge to the immune system at all.**

Serum or gamma globulins from someone already immune → given to a non-immune person.

**Use:** immediate protection against acute infections — **tetanus, measles**

---

## Active immunization

The immune system is **activated** and makes its own antibodies.

- **Natural** — you caught the disease (clinical *or* subclinical)
- **Artificial** — **vaccines or toxoids**

---

## Vaccines — the basics

**A vaccine** = dead pathogens, or live but **attenuated** (artificially weakened) organisms.

It works by producing **antibodies** or activating **T-lymphocytes**.

**Vaccination** = the process of distributing and administering it.

### History worth remembering

- **Smallpox** — most feared; **20–30% case fatality**; survivors scarred for life
- **Variolation** — practised from the **10th century AD**
- **Edward Jenner** — first to publish, **1796**; made **smallpox vaccine from cowpox virus** — the **first live vaccine**
- **Louis Pasteur** — rabies vaccine, **100 years later**

---

## Live attenuated vs Inactivated — the key comparison

| | **LIVE ATTENUATED** | **INACTIVATED** |
|---|---|---|
| How made | Modify wild organism by **repeated culturing** | **Heat and/or chemicals (formalin)** |
| Replicates? | **Yes** | **No — cannot cause disease** |
| Response | **Like natural infection** | Mostly **humoral** |
| Circulating antibody | **Interferes** | **Less interference** |
| Doses | **One usually enough** | **3–5** |
| Duration | **Long lasting** | **Titres diminish with time** |

---

**Live attenuated VIRUSES:** polio, measles, rubella, mumps, rabies, varicella, yellow fever, rotavirus, influenza

**Live attenuated BACTERIA:** **BCG**, typhoid

---

**Inactivated VIRAL:** polio (**Salk**), hepatitis A, rabies, influenza

**Inactivated BACTERIAL:** pertussis, typhoid, cholera, plague, anthrax

---

### Fractional vaccines

- **Subunit** (single protein) — hepatitis B, acellular pertussis, HPV
- **Toxoids** — **diphtheria, tetanus**
- **Polysaccharide** — pneumococcal, meningococcal, *Salmonella typhi*
- **Conjugate polysaccharide** — **Hib**, pneumococcal, meningococcal
- **Recombinant** (DNA technology) — hepatitis B, HPV, influenza

---

## Immune globulins — four types

| Type | From | Used for |
|---|---|---|
| **Immune globulin** | Pooled from **many adults** | Hepatitis A, measles |
| **Homologous human hyperimmune globulin** | Humans with **high specific antibody** | PEP: hepatitis B, **rabies, tetanus**, varicella |
| **Heterologous hyperimmune serum (antitoxin)** | **Animals** | **Botulism, diphtheria** |
| **Monoclonal antibodies** | A **clone** of antibody-producing cells | **RSV — palivizumab** |

---

## What determines how well a vaccine works?

**Vaccine factors:** chemical and physical state · route/site · dose · immunogenicity

**Host factors:** age · sex · nutrition · **pre-existing antibodies** · genetics · immune status · pregnancy

---

## Five classes of protective antibody

- **Antitoxins** — inactivate soluble toxic proteins
- **Opsonins** — facilitate phagocytosis
- **Lysins** — interact with serum complement
- **Neutralising** — prevent viral proliferation
- **Antiadhesins** — prevent adhesion to mucosal surfaces

---

## Why we vaccinate — beyond the individual

> **Not just individual protection.**

- Decrease **morbidity and mortality**
- Increase **HERD IMMUNITY**

**Herd immunity** = the resistance of a **group** to invasion and spread of an infectious agent, due to immunity of a **large proportion** of the group.

> **High coverage is needed** to achieve it.

---

## Delivery methods

Routine immunization · response to an epidemic · supplemental immunization activities · **mop up** immunization · mobile or fixed locations

---

## Special circumstances

**Pneumococcal polysaccharide vaccine:** SCA · anatomic asplenia · nephrotic syndrome · renal failure · HIV · organ transplant

**HIV:**

- **Asymptomatic → ALL vaccines**
- **Symptomatic → BCG and yellow fever CONTRAINDICATED**
- **IPV preferred to OPV**

> **Live vaccines are generally not given to immunocompromised children.**

---

## The three lists you must not confuse

### CONTRAINDICATIONS — do not give

- **Anaphylaxis**
- **Encephalopathy**
- Progressive neurologic disorders
- Immunodeficiency disorder; immunosuppressive therapy
- Pregnancy
- Any illness requiring hospital admission → **defer**

### PRECAUTIONS — caution, weigh it up

- Fever **> 40.5 °C**
- Collapse / shock
- Inconsolable crying
- Guillain–Barré syndrome
- **For MMR:** blood transfusion · thrombocytopenia

### NOT CONTRAINDICATIONS — give the vaccine

- URTI
- **Mild** fever
- **Diarrhoea — but repeat the dose**
- Cerebral palsy
- Breastfeeding
- Antimicrobial therapy

> These six are the commonest reasons children are wrongly turned away.

---

## Adverse reactions

Pain, swelling, redness, soreness · fever · **anaphylaxis** · **encephalopathy (convulsions, coma)** · rash/urticaria

---

## Cost effectiveness

Savings on treatment cost · ↓mortality · ↓morbidity and disability · avoidance of intangible costs (suffering) · external/spillover benefits

> **Smallpox eradication saved 32 billion dollars.** Also: poliomyelitis eradication, Hib immunization.

---

## Coverage and missed opportunities

**Immunization coverage** = percentage of the target population that have received immunization.

- Often evaluated in **children aged 12–23 months**

**Missed opportunity** = a child **with no contraindication** does not get a vaccine **he is eligible for**, when he visits a health facility.

**Five causes:**

- Vaccine not available
- **Not checking immune status**
- **Failure to give simultaneous injections**
- Failure to give info to parents
- **Failure to immunize every day**

---

## COLD CHAIN

**Definition:** a system of storage and transport of vaccines at **low temperatures**, from the manufacturer to the actual vaccination site.

**Why?** Because **vaccine failure may occur** from failure of storage and transport under strict temperature control.

---

### The split that gets examined

| **FREEZER** | **COLD compartment — NEVER freeze** |
|---|---|
| **Polio** | **BCG · DPT · DT · TT · Typhoid · DILUENTS** |
| **Measles** | |

> Memory: the **live viral** ones (polio, measles) can freeze. **Everything else must not.**

Also: **protect from sunlight** and keep away from **antiseptics**.

---

### The numbers

- Health centre storage: most vaccines (**except polio**) up to **5 weeks**
- Refrigerator: strictly **4 °C to 8 °C**
- **Opened vial WITH preservative → 3 hours**
- **Opened vial WITHOUT preservative → 1 hour**

---

### Cold chain equipment

Walk-in cold rooms · deep freezer · small deep freezer · cold boxes · vaccine carrier · day carrier · ice packs
