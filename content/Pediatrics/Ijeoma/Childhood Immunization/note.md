## Introduction

**Immunization** is defined as the **procedure by which the body is prepared to fight against specific disease**.

It is used to **induce the immune resistance of the body to a specific disease**.

Immunization is of two types:

- **Passive** immunization
- **Active** immunization

## Passive immunization

Passive immunization is produced **without challenging the immune system of the body**.

It is produced by **administration of serum or gamma globulins from a person who is already immunized** (affected by the disease) **to a non-immune person**.

Passive immunization is acquired either **naturally or artificially**.

### Passive natural immunization

Acquired from the **mother before and after birth**.

- **Before birth** — immunity is transferred from mother to fetus in the form of maternal antibodies (**mainly IgG**) **through the placenta**
- **After birth** — antibodies (**IgA**) are transferred through **breast milk**

### Passive artificial immunization

Developed by **injecting previously preformed antibodies** using **serum from humans or animals**.

This type of immunity is useful for providing **immediate protection against acute infections** like **tetanus, measles**, etc.

## Active immunization

Active immunization or immunity is acquired by **activating the immune system of the body**.

The body develops resistance against disease by **producing antibodies following exposure to antigens**. Active immunity is acquired either **naturally or artificially**.

### Active natural immunization

Involves activation of the immune system in the body to produce antibodies. It is achieved in **both clinical and subclinical infections**.

### Active artificial immunization

Achieved by the **administration of vaccines or toxoids**.

## Vaccine and vaccination

**Vaccine** is a **substance that is introduced into the body to prevent the disease produced by certain pathogens**.

A vaccine consists of **dead pathogens** or **live but attenuated (artificially weakened) organisms**. The vaccine induces immunity against the pathogen, either by **production of antibodies** or by **activation of T-lymphocytes**.

> **Edward Jenner produced the first live vaccine.** He produced the vaccine for **smallpox from cowpox virus**.

**Vaccination** is the **process of distributing and administering vaccine**.

## Historical perspectives

- **Smallpox** — most feared; **20–30% case fatality rates**; leaving survivors **scarred for life**
- **Variolation** practised as early as the **10th century AD**
- **Edward Jenner** — first to publish, in **1796**
- **Rabies vaccine by Louis Pasteur**, 100 years later

## Immunizing agents

**VACCINE:** any preparation which, when administered, is able to **stimulate the immune system to produce specific responses that inactivate, destroy or suppress a given pathogen**.

Two categories:

- **Live attenuated**
- **Inactivated**

### Live attenuated vaccines

- Produced by **modifying a naturally occurring (wild) organism**, usually by **repeated culturing**
- **Retains the ability to replicate** and produce an immune response **similar to natural infection**
- **Circulating antibody may interfere** with the response
- **Long lasting immunity**
- **One dose usually suffices**

**Examples — live attenuated viruses:** poliomyelitis, measles, rubella, mumps, rabies, varicella, yellow fever, rotavirus, influenza

**Examples — live attenuated bacteria:** Bacille Calmette-Guérin (BCG), typhoid

### Inactivated vaccines

- The organism is **inactivated with heat and/or chemicals (formalin)**. With **fractional** vaccines it is further treated to **purify only those components** to be included in the vaccine
- **Non-replicating**, so **cannot cause disease**
- **Less interference from circulating antibodies**
- **Requires more doses — 3–5**
- **Mostly humoral immune response**
- **Antibody titres diminish with time**

**Examples — viral:** poliomyelitis (Salk), hepatitis A, rabies, influenza

**Examples — bacterial:** pertussis, typhoid, cholera, plague, anthrax

**Fractional:**

- **Subunit** (made from a single protein) — hepatitis B, acellular pertussis, human papilloma virus
- **Toxoids** — diphtheria, tetanus
- **Polysaccharide** — pneumococcal, meningococcal, *Salmonella typhi*
- **Conjugate polysaccharide** — Hib, pneumococcal, meningococcal
- **Recombinant** (from recombinant DNA technology) — hepatitis B, human papilloma virus, influenza

### Immune globulins

- **Immune globulin** — homologous pooled antibody, from many adults; contains antibodies to many antigens. Used for **hepatitis A, measles**
- **Homologous human hyperimmune globulin** — from humans with high levels of specific antibodies. Used for **post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) of hepatitis B, rabies, tetanus, varicella**
- **Heterologous hyperimmune serum (antitoxin)** — from **animals**. Used for **botulism, diphtheria**
- **Monoclonal antibodies** — derived from a clone of antibody-producing cells. **RSV — palivizumab**

## Determinants of immune response

**Vaccine factors:**

- Chemical and physical state of the vaccine
- Route/site of administration
- Vaccine dose
- Immunogenicity of the vaccine

**Host factors:**

- Age
- Sex
- Nutrition
- Pre-existing antibodies
- Genetics
- Immune status
- Pregnancy

## Classes of protective antibody

- **Antitoxins** — inactivate soluble toxic protein products
- **Opsonins** — facilitate phagocytosis
- **Lysins** — interact with components of serum complement
- **Neutralising antibodies** — prevent proliferation of infectious virus
- **Antiadhesins** — prevent adhesion to mucosal surfaces

## Vaccine recommendation

**Factors to consider:**

- Epidemiology — incidence, age, sex
- Age-specific morbidity and mortality
- Risk of adverse events
- Cost effectiveness
- Age of recommended health care visits
- Efficacy of the vaccine

Vaccinations are generally recommended at the age of:

- **Significant risk of disease and complications**
- **Protective immunologic response**

Also recommended for **special situations**, e.g. sickle cell anaemia (SCA), HIV.

## Objectives of the immunization programme

- **Not just individual protection**
- **Decrease morbidity and mortality** from target diseases
- **Increase herd immunity** — the **resistance of a group to invasion and spread of an infectious agent, due to immunity of a large proportion of the group**
- **High coverage is needed** to achieve this

## Vaccine delivery

- Routine immunization
- Response to an epidemic
- Supplemental immunization activities
- Mop up immunization
- Mobile or fixed locations

## Immunization in special circumstances

**Pneumococcal polysaccharide vaccine** — SCA, anatomic asplenia, nephrotic syndrome, renal failure, HIV, organ transplant

**HIV:**

- **All vaccines if asymptomatic**
- **BCG and yellow fever contraindicated if symptomatic**
- **IPV preferred to OPV**

Also: cerebrospinal meningitis, rabies.

> **Live vaccines are generally not given to immunocompromised children.**

## Contraindications

- Anaphylaxis
- Encephalopathy
- Progressive neurologic disorders
- Immunodeficiency disorder; immunosuppressive therapy
- Pregnancy
- Any illness requiring admission to hospital — **defer** immunization

## Precautions

- Fever > 40.5 °C
- Collapse / shock
- Inconsolable crying
- Guillain–Barré syndrome

**For MMR:**

- Blood transfusion
- Thrombocytopenia

## NOT contraindications

- Upper respiratory tract infection
- Mild fever
- **Diarrhoea — repeat dose**
- Cerebral palsy
- Breastfeeding
- Antimicrobial therapy

## Adverse reactions

- Pain, swelling, redness, soreness
- Fever
- Anaphylaxis
- Encephalopathy — convulsions, coma
- Rash / urticaria

## Cost effectiveness

- Savings on treatment cost
- ↓ mortality
- ↓ morbidity and disability
- Avoidance of intangible costs (suffering)
- External / spillover benefits

**Examples:**

- **Smallpox eradication — saving 32 billion dollars**
- Poliomyelitis eradication
- Hib immunization

## Immunization coverage

The **percentage of the target population that have received immunization**.

- Often evaluated in **children aged 12–23 months** — estimates recent immunization performance
- May be evaluated in the target population for a given vaccine, e.g. under-fives following NIDs

## Missed opportunity

Occurs when an individual **in whom there is no contraindication does not receive the vaccine(s) for which he/she is eligible when he/she visits a health facility**. Can occur during curative or preventive visits.

**Causes of missed opportunities:**

- Non-availability of vaccine
- Not checking immune status
- Failure to give simultaneous injections
- Failure to provide info to parents
- Failure to immunize every day

## Cold chain

The **cold chain is a system of storage and transport of vaccines at low temperatures, by the manufacturer to the actual vaccination site**.

The cold chain system is necessary because **vaccine failure may occur due to failure of storage and transport under strict temperature controls**.

- Vaccines stored in the **freezer compartment**: **polio and measles**
- Vaccines which must be stored in the **cold compartment and never allowed to freeze**: **BCG, DPT, DT, TT, typhoid, and diluents**
- Vaccines must be **protected from sunlight** and prevented from **contact with antiseptics**

At health centres, most vaccines (**except polio**) can be stored up to **5 weeks**, if the refrigerator temperature is strictly kept between **4 °C and 8 °C**.

**Storage of opened vial:**

- **With preservatives — up to 3 hours**
- **Without preservatives — up to 1 hour**

### Cold chain equipment

- Walk-in cold rooms
- Deep freezer
- Small deep freezer
- Cold boxes
- Vaccine carrier
- Day carrier
- Ice packs
