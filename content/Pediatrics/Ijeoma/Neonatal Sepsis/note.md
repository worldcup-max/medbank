## Introduction

Neonatal sepsis is **one of the leading causes of neonatal mortality** and a **major public health problem**, especially in developing countries.

Even though recent medical advances have improved neonatal care, many challenges remain in the diagnosis and management of neonatal infections.

The diagnosis is complicated by:

- The frequent presence of **non-infectious conditions that resemble sepsis**, especially in preterm infants (e.g. RDS, CHD)
- The **absence of optimal/definitive diagnostic tests**
- **Viral and fungal infections** that occur in newborns and **mimic bacterial infections**

Since neonatal sepsis is a high-risk disease, especially in preterm infants, clinicians are compelled to **empirically administer antibiotics** to infants with risk factors and/or signs of suspected sepsis — the **"RULE OUT SEPSIS SYNDROME" (ROSS)**.

### Burden

- NNS is the **3rd leading cause of neonatal mortality**, after **asphyxia** and **LBW/prematurity**
- The **top 4 diagnoses** in most newborn units are **asphyxia, NNS, preterm/LBW and jaundice**
- **13%** of all neonatal mortality
- **42%** of deaths in the first week of life
- Many cases are **not reported**, especially in developing countries
- NNS explains about **a quarter (25%) of neonatal deaths in Nigeria**

### The definition problem

- **Lack of a widely accepted and consensus definition**
- Definition relates to **clinical features and outcome of investigations**
- Lack of definitive accepted criteria that **cut across term and preterm** manifestations
- Both **broad-spectrum antibiotics** and **prolonged empirical treatment** are associated with **adverse outcomes**

**Risks of overuse of antibiotics:**

- Increased **antibiotic resistance**
- Negative effects of **altering the microbiome**
- Increased rate of serious **necrotising enterocolitis**
- Increased **mortality**

## Definitions

**Bacteraemia** — the presence of **viable bacteria in the blood**.

**Septicaemia** — **systemic infection confirmed by the presence of actively multiplying bacteria in the blood**.

**Neonatal sepsis (NNS)** — a **clinical syndrome characterised by systemic signs of infection** (the infant is looking ill), **accompanied by bacteraemia, in the first 28 days of life**.

### Diagnostic categories

**Suspected sepsis** — regardless of whether there is a clinical symptom or not, the **presence of sepsis risk factors** in the baby, or findings suggesting sepsis on follow-up.

**Clinical sepsis** — clinical and laboratory findings are present, but there is **failure to show the causative microorganism**.

**Proven sepsis** — clinical and laboratory findings are present, **plus demonstration of the pathogenic microorganism in cultures taken from a sterile field**; or a **positive blood culture or PCR** in the presence of clinical signs and symptoms of infection.

> For **coagulase-negative sepsis**: **two positive blood cultures**, or **one positive blood culture plus a positive CRP**.

**Probable sepsis** — presence of signs and symptoms of infection **plus at least two abnormal laboratory results** when blood culture is negative.

**Possible sepsis** — presence of clinical signs and symptoms of infection **plus raised CRP or IL-6/IL-8 level** when blood culture is negative.

## SIRS — how relevant to neonatal sepsis?

**SIRS** (Systemic Inflammatory Response Syndrome) is the **physiologic response to inflammation from any source, including infection**.

So: **Sepsis = SIRS + focus of infection**.

**BUT** many neonates with sepsis **lack a specific focus and signs**, especially preterms. However, SIRS still finds its use.

Strictly, diagnosis of SIRS requires that **temperature or leukocyte abnormalities** be present.

### SIRS criteria — presence of 2 of the following

At least **two** of the following four criteria, **one of which must be abnormal temperature or leukocyte count**:

1. Core temperature of **> 38.5 °C or < 36 °C**
2. **Tachycardia** — mean heart rate **2 SD above normal for age** in the absence of external stimulus
3. **Bradycardia** — mean heart rate **< 10th centile** (provided no external vagal stimulus, beta-blockers, or CHD), or unexplained bradycardia of **> 30 min**
4. **Mean respiratory rate 2 SD above normal for age** (40–60 c/min)
5. **Leukocyte count elevated or depressed for age**, or **10% immature neutrophils**

> SIRS has not made early diagnosis of NNS realizable, because of the absence of specific symptoms and the suboptimal diagnostic value of the laboratory findings.

## Severe sepsis and septic shock

**Severe sepsis** = sepsis **plus one** of:

- **Cardiovascular organ dysfunction**
- **Acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS)**
- **≥ 2 organ dysfunctions** (respiratory, renal, neurologic, haematologic, or hepatic)

Or: sepsis **+ signs of end organ damage OR hypoperfusion**.

**Signs of end organ damage:**

- Low PaO₂/FiO₂ (acute lung injury)
- Lack of bowel sounds (ileus)
- Oliguria
- Increased total bilirubin
- Thrombocytopenia

**Signs of hypoperfusion / cardiovascular dysfunction:**

- Increased lactic acid
- Low blood pressure
- Decreased capillary refill
- Change in mental status

**Septic shock** = severe sepsis **plus sepsis-induced hypotension that is refractory to fluid resuscitation**.

## Epidemiology

Incidence varies:

- Across regions
- Across centres within the same region
- From time to time within the same centre

Other points:

- **Commoner in developing than developed nations**
- **Worse in preterms than terms**
- **Prevalent organisms and antibiotic sensitivity also vary from time to time**

## Types of neonatal sepsis

### In developed countries (3 types)

1. **Early onset NNS** — symptoms **< 72 hours** (various cut-offs 48 hr – 7 days, but most epidemiological studies use 72 hours)
2. **Late onset NNS** — cases diagnosed on the **4th–30th days of life**
3. **Very late onset NNS** — cases diagnosed in infants hospitalised in the NICU from the first 30 days of life until discharge

### Early versus late onset

- **Early onset** disease pathogens are related to the **maternal genital tract**, with **Group B Streptococcus (GBS)**, and the delivery area
- **Late onset** disease pathogens reflect **contamination at a later date and nosocomial infection**

### In developing countries

Unlike in developed nations, in developing nations **similar organisms are usually found before and after 72 hours**.

- Most deliveries at home, by TBAs, and even in maternity homes/centres, are **infected with community-acquired pathogens**, not just maternal pathogens
- Most deliveries also occur in **unclean areas with unclean delivery practices**, leading to infections with **nosocomial organisms early in life**

Hence the classification into:

- **Community acquired**
- **Hospital acquired**

## Aetiology

### Developed countries

**Early onset sepsis (EOS):** Group B Streptococcus **43–58%**, *E. coli*, other gram-negative bacteria, *S. aureus*, coagulase-negative *Staphylococcus*, *L. monocytogenes*

**Late onset sepsis (LOS):** coagulase-negative *Staphylococcus*, *E. coli*, *Klebsiella* sp., *S. aureus*, *Candida albicans*, *Enterococcus* sp., *P. aeruginosa*, other *Candida* sp.

### Developing countries

**Community acquired:** *Klebsiella* sp., *S. aureus*, *E. coli*, Group B Streptococcus, *S. pneumoniae*, *Salmonella* sp.

**Hospital acquired:** *Klebsiella* sp., *S. aureus*, *E. coli*, coagulase-negative *Staphylococcus*, *Pseudomonas* sp., *Enterobacter* sp., *Candida* sp.

## Risk and host factors

### Maternal — MAJOR

- **ROM > 18 hr / PROM**
- **Intrapartum maternal fever** (> 38.0 °C / > 100.4 °F)
- **Chorioamnionitis**
- **Malodorous liquor**
- **Sustained fetal heart rate > 160 beats/min**

### Fetal / neonatal

Congenital malformations, and **preterm/LBW especially VLBW and ELBW**:

- Ectopia vesicae
- Neural tube defects
- Cutis aplasia
- Exomphalos / omphalocoele / gastroschisis
- Obstructive problems (GIT, GUT, CVS, etc.)

### Environmental

Home, TBA, road/taxi/casualty deliveries; nursery and environmental circumstances and practices (poor soap and water use, poor spacing); **overcrowding**; **poor, inadequate or infrequent hand washing**.

### MINOR risk factors

- Peri/intrapartum maternal fever (> 37.5 °C but < 38.5 °C)
- Twin gestation
- Prematurity (< 37 weeks)
- Maternal WBC > 15,000
- **ROM > 12 hr**
- Tachypnoea (< 1 hour) — increased RDS is symptomatic
- **Maternal GBS colonization**
- Low APGAR (< 5 at 1 min)
- Mode of delivery
- Instrumentation and manipulations
- Family history of a sibling with systemic bacterial disease under 3 months of age
- Urinary tract infection
- Maternal substance abuse
- History of recurrent abortion
- Poor maternal nutrition

### Labour characteristics

- Preterm labour
- Premature or prolonged time between rupture of membranes and delivery
- Fetal tachycardia without maternal fever, blood loss, hypotension, or tachycardia-inducing medication
- Foul lochia
- Rupture of membranes > 12 hours

### Infant characteristics

- **Apgar < 6 at 5 minutes**
- Meconium-stained amniotic fluid
- Oxygen requirement
- Respiratory distress
- Neutropenia
- **Male sex**
- Congenital anomalies that cause breakdown of anatomic barriers to infection
- Polymorphonuclear leucocytes and intracellular organisms in gastric aspirate

> The above portend increased risk of systemic bacterial infections in the newborn infant during the **first seven days of life**.

## Stages of sepsis development — the seed-soil principle

**Skin/mucosa contamination → invasion of skin/mucosa → multiplication of organisms → demonstration of immunocompetence and antibody responses**

## Susceptibility of the newborn to infection

Both **specific and non-specific** mechanisms are **poorly developed**.

**Non-specific mechanisms:**

- **Skin and mucosal barriers are weak and fragile**
- **Neutrophil and monocyte response (chemotaxis) is slow and inadequate**
- **Poor opsonin support**
- **Hypocomplementaemia**
- Intracellular killing of ingested bacteria is weak

**Deficient host immunity — poor intracellular killing mechanisms:**

- **Oxygen-dependent mechanisms** (OH⁻, O⁻ formation) are poorly developed
- **Non-oxygen dependent mechanisms** are also poorly developed (lysosomal enzymes)
- **Specific immunity — humoral and cellular — are weak and ineffective**
- **Relative hypo-immunoglobulinaemia**

## Diagnosis

- Specific and non-specific signs and symptoms
- **Have a high index of suspicion**
- **Sepsis score**
- **SOFA score**
- **Töllner sepsis score**
- Paediatric Committee (PDCO) of the European Medicines Agency (EMA) score
- **Sepsis calculators for EOS**

## Clinical features

- Symptoms and signs can be **rapidly progressive**
- **Easily involves multiple organs/systems**
- Early clinical signs are **non-specific, non-localizing, subtle**, do not distinguish among organisms, and are often not organ specific — e.g. meningitis presenting with respiratory distress or diarrhoea

**General:** fever, temperature instability, poor colour, dusky colour, "not doing well", poor feeding, sclerema, oedema

**Gastrointestinal:** poor feeding, poor swallowing/sucking, abdominal distension, vomiting, diarrhoea, water brash/excessive salivation and secretions, feed intolerance, increased feed aspirate, hepatomegaly

**Respiratory:** respiratory distress; increased nasal secretions; dyspnoea (flaring, chest retractions/recession); tachypnoea; bradypnoea/apnoea; noisy breathing (worsening stridor, grunting, wheezing); cheek puffing; cyanosis

**Renal:** oliguria, haematuria, dribbling

**Cardiovascular:** pallor, dusky colour, mottling; cold clammy skin; tachycardia, bradycardia, cyanosis, hypotension, oedema, dysrhythmias

**Central nervous system:** poor activity, weakness, excessive sleep or poor sleep, apnoea, lethargy, irritability, tremors, seizures, reduced primitive reflexes (poor sucking, swallowing, poor rooting, abnormal Moro), hyporeflexia, hypotonia, irregular respiration, full fontanelle, high-pitched cry

**Haematologic:** jaundice, pallor, petechiae, purpura, bleeding, ecchymosis, splenomegaly

**Metabolic:** unexplained hypoglycaemia, hyperglycaemia, acidosis (respiratory, metabolic), hyponatraemia

**Others:** organ-specific symptoms, e.g. eye discharges and swelling/oedema; ear discharges; limb swelling; umbilical redness, foul smelling, discharges

## Community diagnosis of sepsis

WHO identified **seven clinical signs** as indication for neonatal sepsis, for immediate referral:

1. Difficulty feeding
2. Convulsions
3. Movement only when stimulated
4. Respiratory rate > 60 per minute
5. Severe chest indrawing
6. Axillary temperature > 37.5 °C or < 35.5 °C
7. Cyanosis and grunting

## Suspicious clinical signs

- **Petechiae and purpura** in the setting of haemodynamic instability
- Fever
- Cough
- **Hypoxaemia** in the setting of leukocytosis and pulmonary infiltrates
- **Distended tympanitic abdomen** with fever and leukocytosis associated with a perforated bowel

## Criteria for diagnosis — clinical variables

- Temperature instability
- **Heart rate ≥ 180 beats/min or ≤ 100 beats/min**
- **Respiratory rate > 40 breaths/min** plus grunting or desaturations
- Lethargy / altered mental status
- **Glucose intolerance** (plasma glucose > 10 mmol/L)
- Feed intolerance

## Haemodynamic and tissue perfusion variables

**Haemodynamic:**

- Blood pressure **2 SD below normal for age**
- **Systolic pressure < 50 mmHg** (newborn day 1)
- **Systolic pressure < 65 mmHg** (infants ≤ 1 month)

**Tissue perfusion:**

- **Capillary refill > 3 seconds**
- **Plasma lactate > 3 mmol/L**

## Differential diagnosis

NNS may **coexist with** other pathologies and may also **mimic** other abnormalities.

**Neurologic:** hypoxic ischaemic encephalopathy, neonatal seizure, intracranial haemorrhage

**Respiratory:** respiratory distress syndrome (especially preterm), aspiration pneumonia (amniotic fluid or gastric contents), tracheo-oesophageal fistula, transient tachypnoea of the newborn

**Cardiovascular:** congenital hypoplastic left heart syndrome and other structural diseases; persistent pulmonary hypertension of the newborn; acquired — myocarditis, hypovolaemic or cardiogenic shock

**Gastrointestinal:** necrotizing enterocolitis, spontaneous GI perforations

**Metabolic:** hypoglycaemia; adrenal disorders (adrenal haemorrhage and/or insufficiency, CAH); inborn errors of metabolism (organic acidurias, lactic acidosis, urea cycle disorder)

**Haematologic:** vitamin K clotting factor deficiency, neonatal purpura fulminans, immune mediated thrombocytopenia, immune mediated neutropenia, anaemia from other causes, haemolytic disorders (ABO, Rh isoimmunization, G6PD deficiency), other hereditary clotting disorders

## Principles of management

1. **Review clinical features. Have a high index of suspicion. Balance between risks and features seen**
2. **Investigate and take necessary culture samples**
3. **Commence antibiotic treatment immediately**, especially with major risk factors
4. **Review treatment with clinical progress and laboratory results**
5. **Repeat investigations when necessary** — sudden deterioration may occur

## Investigations

### Sepsis screen

- Full blood count
- Blood cultures
- CSF examination
- Urine cultures
- Culture of other relevant body fluids or effluents
- **Chest radiograph if indicated**

### Complete blood count — findings suggestive of sepsis

- White blood cell count **decreased < 5,000/mm³**
- White blood cell count **increased > 34,000/mm³**
- **Absolute neutrophil < 1,000/mm³**
- **Bands to total neutrophil count ratio > 0.2**
- **Immature to mature neutrophil count ratio > 0.2**

### Urine

**Urinalysis and urine culture** — indicated for **late onset sepsis**; **not useful in early onset sepsis**.

### Töllner score

Combined clinical and laboratory findings:

- **< 5 — normal**
- **5–10 — suspected**
- **> 10 points — definite sepsis**

### Other ancillary investigations

- **Neutrophil band to total neutrophil ratio** — normal < 0.16 (12 hr of life) and < 0.12 after 60 hr
- Elevated **micro erythrocyte sedimentation rate**
- Elevated **C-reactive protein**
- Elevated **high sensitivity CRP (hs-CRP) > 1 mg/L**
- Elevated **procalcitonin > 2–2.5 ng/mL** (after postnatal 72 hours)
- Haptoglobulin
- Buffy coat smear
- **Cytokines and chemokines** — CD11b, CD64, IL-6, IL-8 as markers of inflammation; also monokines, RANTES, MCP-1 and IP-10
- **Serum Amyloid A (SAA)**
- **Lipopolysaccharide Binding Protein (LBP)**

### Other laboratory findings

- **Immature neutrophils > 10%**
- **Immature:total neutrophil ratio > 0.2**
- **Thrombocytopenia < 100,000 × 10⁹/L**
- **CRP > 10 mg/L**, or 2 SD above normal value
- **Procalcitonin > 8.1 mg/dL**, or 2 SD above normal value
- **IL-6 or IL-8 > 70 pg/mL**
- **16S PCR positive**

### Other investigations

- Polymerase chain reaction techniques to recover bacteria
- Latex agglutination tests
- Clotting profile
- Liver function test — to rule out bacterial hepatitis
- Blood glucose
- Electrolytes
- **Renal function — because of aminoglycosides**

### Blood culture and other samples

- **Blood culture is positive in only 5–10% of neonatal sepsis**
- **Arterial blood gas** — indicated for signs or symptoms of hypoxia
- **Lumbar puncture** — indications include primary diagnosis of sepsis; positive blood culture; and neurological signs or symptoms

### Superficial swab cultures

Cultures obtained from superficial regions — axilla, umbilical cord, outer ear canal, nasopharynx and orogastric tubes — show **poor correlation**.

> **Routine collection of superficial swab cultures is NOT recommended** in neonatal sepsis.

> **Tracheal aspirate cultures are NOT recommended** in prolonged intubation, due to rapid colonization following intubation.

> **Urine culture is NOT recommended in EOS.**

## Antibiotic therapy

**Rational use of antibiotics:** the **correct use of quality antibiotics, in the right dosage, with wide microbial coverage, for the right duration, via the right mode of delivery, for the right patient**.

Practices vary across Nigeria, ranging from **ampicillin/gentamicin combinations** through **2nd to 3rd generation cephalosporins**.

Nosocomial infections are often treated with **vancomycin or fluoroquinolones + amikacin**.

> However, resistance of *Klebsiella* spp. to **gentamicin is about 60–72%**, and to **3rd generation cephalosporins 57–66%**.

**Monitor response with:** clinical state, CRP, procalcitonin, immature/total neutrophil levels.

**Choice of initial antibiotic(s) is based on:**

- **Sensitivity pattern in the local setting**
- Review of response of the patient, especially where laboratory evaluation is poor

**WHO Committee Recommendation:**

- **First line:** penicillin (benzylpenicillin/ampicillin/amoxicillin) + aminoglycoside (gentamicin), then vancomycin/cloxacillin + aminoglycoside (amikacin)
- **Second line:** penicillin/cephalosporin (cefotaxime/ceftriaxone) + aminoglycoside (amikacin)

## Supportive management

- Correction of **anaemia, electrolyte and acid/base imbalances**
- **Prevent hypoxia and hypoglycaemia/hyperglycaemia**
- **Maintain a thermoneutral environment**
- **Exchange blood transfusion, buffy coat transfusion** — for sclerema, bacterial hepatitis with high conjugated bilirubin, leucopenia
- Treatment with **recombinant cytokines**
- **Management of hypotension and shock**

### Other ancillary treatments

Treatments that increase the number or function of neutrophils — **granulocyte transfusions, GM-CSF, intravenous immune globulin (IVIG)** — are of **doubtful use** in neonatal sepsis.

**Pentoxifylline**, which works by **reducing TNF-α concentrations** associated with sepsis and **improving microcirculation**, showed **better survival**.

## Prevention

- Minimise/reduce risk factors
- **Intrapartum penicillin prophylaxis 2–4 hours before SVD**, especially in culture-positive mothers for GBS — significantly reduces EOS
- **Good ANC** to avoid preterm/LBW and asphyxiated deliveries
- **Regular hand washing** to reduce skin/mucosa contamination
- **Eye care with 0.5% erythromycin eye ointment stat**
- **Cord care with 4% chlorhexidine gel once daily for 5–7 days**
- **Deliver baby on mother's abdomen for skin-to-skin contact for the critical 1 hour**
- **Early and exclusive breastfeeding**
- **Reduce traffic flow in the newborn unit**
- **Personalize facilities** among the newborns on admission
- Sepsis control in the newborn unit
- **Rational use of antibiotics**
