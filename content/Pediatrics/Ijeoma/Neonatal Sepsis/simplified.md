# Neonatal Sepsis — Simplified

**One line:** a newborn with almost no immune defence gets bacteria in the blood — and the signs are so vague that you must treat on suspicion.

---

## Why this topic is hard (and why that matters)

Four problems, stated in the introduction:

- **Non-infectious conditions mimic sepsis** — RDS, congenital heart disease, especially in preterms
- **There is no definitive diagnostic test**
- **Viral and fungal infections mimic bacterial ones**
- **There is no consensus definition**

> So clinicians give antibiotics **empirically** to any baby with risk factors or suspicious signs. That approach has a name: **"Rule Out Sepsis Syndrome" (ROSS)**.

---

## But over-treating has real costs

Broad-spectrum and prolonged empirical antibiotics cause:

- **Antibiotic resistance**
- **Altered microbiome**
- **Increased necrotising enterocolitis**
- **Increased mortality**

> **The whole topic is a balance:** miss sepsis and the baby dies; over-treat and you cause harm.

---

## The numbers

- **3rd leading cause** of neonatal mortality — after **asphyxia** and **LBW/prematurity**
- Top 4 diagnoses in newborn units: **asphyxia · NNS · preterm/LBW · jaundice**
- **13%** of all neonatal mortality
- **42%** of deaths in the first week of life
- **25%** — a quarter — of neonatal deaths in **Nigeria**

---

## Definitions — get these exact

**Bacteraemia** = viable bacteria **in the blood**

**Septicaemia** = systemic infection confirmed by **actively multiplying** bacteria in blood

**Neonatal sepsis** = a **clinical syndrome** of systemic signs of infection (the infant looks ill) **+ bacteraemia**, in the **first 28 days**

---

### The five diagnostic categories

| Category | What it means |
|---|---|
| **Suspected** | Risk factors present — with or without symptoms |
| **Clinical** | Clinical **and** lab findings, but **organism not shown** |
| **Proven** | Clinical + lab + **organism grown from a sterile site** (or positive PCR) |
| **Probable** | Symptoms + **≥2 abnormal labs**, culture negative |
| **Possible** | Symptoms + **raised CRP or IL-6/IL-8**, culture negative |

> **Coagulase-negative sepsis needs more:** two positive blood cultures, **or** one positive culture **plus** a positive CRP. (Because it is a common contaminant.)

---

## SIRS — and why it disappoints in neonates

**Sepsis = SIRS + focus of infection**

**SIRS criteria — need 2 of 4, and one MUST be temperature or leukocyte count:**

- Core temperature **> 38.5 °C or < 36 °C**
- **Tachycardia** — 2 SD above normal for age
- **Bradycardia** — < 10th centile, or unexplained bradycardia > 30 min
- **Respiratory rate** 2 SD above normal (40–60/min)
- **Leukocytes** raised or low for age, or **10% immature neutrophils**

> **But it fails in newborns** — because they lack specific symptoms and the lab findings are suboptimal. Many septic neonates, especially preterms, have **no focus at all**.

---

## Severe sepsis and septic shock

**SEVERE SEPSIS** = sepsis + **one** of:

- Cardiovascular organ dysfunction
- **ARDS**
- **≥2 organ dysfunctions** (respiratory, renal, neurologic, haematologic, hepatic)

---

**End organ damage:** low PaO₂/FiO₂ · **no bowel sounds (ileus)** · oliguria · raised bilirubin · thrombocytopenia

**Hypoperfusion:** raised lactate · low BP · **decreased capillary refill** · change in mental status

---

**SEPTIC SHOCK** = severe sepsis + hypotension **refractory to fluid resuscitation**

---

## Types — and why ours are different

### Developed countries

| Type | Timing |
|---|---|
| **Early onset** | **< 72 hours** |
| **Late onset** | **4th–30th day** |
| **Very late onset** | In NICU, from 30 days until discharge |

**Early onset** → organisms from the **maternal genital tract** — especially **GBS** — and the delivery area

**Late onset** → **contamination later, and nosocomial infection**

---

### Developing countries — a different classification

> **The same organisms are found before AND after 72 hours here.**

**Why?**

- Most deliveries are at home, by TBAs, or in maternity centres → **community-acquired** pathogens, not just maternal ones
- Deliveries occur in **unclean areas with unclean practices** → **nosocomial** organisms appear early

So we classify as: **community acquired** vs **hospital acquired**.

---

## Aetiology

**Developed — EOS:** **GBS 43–58%**, *E. coli*, other gram negatives, *S. aureus*, coagulase-negative *Staph*, *L. monocytogenes*

**Developed — LOS:** coagulase-negative *Staph*, *E. coli*, *Klebsiella*, *S. aureus*, *Candida albicans*, *Enterococcus*, *Pseudomonas*

---

**Developing — community acquired:** ***Klebsiella***, *S. aureus*, *E. coli*, GBS, *S. pneumoniae*, *Salmonella*

**Developing — hospital acquired:** ***Klebsiella***, *S. aureus*, *E. coli*, coagulase-negative *Staph*, *Pseudomonas*, *Enterobacter*, *Candida*

> **Note: *Klebsiella* heads both of our lists.** GBS dominates only in developed countries.

---

## Risk factors

### MAJOR maternal — learn these five

- **ROM > 18 hours / PROM**
- **Intrapartum maternal fever > 38.0 °C**
- **Chorioamnionitis**
- **Malodorous liquor**
- **Sustained fetal heart rate > 160/min**

---

### Fetal / neonatal

**Preterm/LBW, especially VLBW and ELBW**, plus congenital malformations that break the skin barrier:

- Ectopia vesicae · neural tube defects · cutis aplasia · exomphalos/gastroschisis · obstructive problems

---

### Environmental

Home, TBA, road/taxi/casualty deliveries · nursery practices (poor soap and water, poor spacing) · **overcrowding** · **poor or infrequent hand washing**

---

### MINOR

Fever 37.5–38.5 °C · twin gestation · prematurity <37/40 · maternal WBC >15,000 · **ROM >12 hr** · tachypnoea · **maternal GBS colonization** · APGAR <5 at 1 min · mode of delivery · instrumentation · sibling with systemic bacterial disease under 3 months · UTI · maternal substance abuse · recurrent abortion · poor maternal nutrition

---

### Infant characteristics

**Apgar <6 at 5 min** · meconium-stained liquor · oxygen requirement · respiratory distress · **neutropenia** · **male sex** · anomalies breaking anatomic barriers · PMN and intracellular organisms in gastric aspirate

> These predict systemic bacterial infection **in the first seven days of life**.

---

## Why newborns are so vulnerable

**Seed-soil principle — the stages:**

> Skin/mucosa contamination → invasion → multiplication of organisms → demonstration of immunocompetence and antibody responses

---

**Non-specific defences are poor:**

- **Skin and mucosal barriers weak and fragile**
- **Chemotaxis slow and inadequate** (neutrophils, monocytes)
- **Poor opsonin support**
- **Hypocomplementaemia**

**Intracellular killing is poor:**

- **Oxygen-dependent** mechanisms (OH⁻, O⁻) poorly developed
- **Non-oxygen dependent** mechanisms poorly developed (lysosomal enzymes)

**Specific immunity is weak:**

- Humoral and cellular immunity weak and ineffective
- **Relative hypo-immunoglobulinaemia**

> **Every layer of defence is defective.** That is why a trivial exposure becomes systemic sepsis.

---

## Clinical features — the golden rule

> Signs are **rapidly progressive**, **involve multiple systems**, and are **non-specific, non-localizing and subtle**.

Meningitis can present as respiratory distress or diarrhoea. **Nothing is where you expect it.**

---

**General:** fever, temperature instability, poor/dusky colour, **"not doing well"**, poor feeding, **sclerema**, oedema

**GIT:** poor feeding, poor sucking/swallowing, abdominal distension, vomiting, diarrhoea, excessive salivation, feed intolerance, increased aspirate, hepatomegaly

**Respiratory:** respiratory distress, nasal secretions, flaring, retractions, tachypnoea, **apnoea**, grunting, stridor, wheeze, cheek puffing, cyanosis

**Renal:** oliguria, haematuria, dribbling

**CVS:** pallor, dusky, **mottling**, cold clammy skin, tachycardia, bradycardia, hypotension, oedema, dysrhythmias

**CNS:** poor activity, weakness, excessive or poor sleep, apnoea, lethargy, irritability, tremors, seizures, **reduced primitive reflexes**, hyporeflexia, hypotonia, **full fontanelle**, **high-pitched cry**

**Haematologic:** jaundice, pallor, **petechiae, purpura**, bleeding, ecchymosis, splenomegaly

**Metabolic:** unexplained **hypoglycaemia**, hyperglycaemia, acidosis, hyponatraemia

---

## The WHO seven signs — for community referral

1. **Difficulty feeding**
2. **Convulsions**
3. **Movement only when stimulated**
4. **Respiratory rate > 60/min**
5. **Severe chest indrawing**
6. **Axillary temperature > 37.5 °C or < 35.5 °C**
7. **Cyanosis and grunting**

> Any one of these → **refer immediately**.

---

## Numbers to know

**Clinical variables:** HR **≥180 or ≤100** · RR **>40** with grunting/desaturation · glucose **>10 mmol/L**

**Haemodynamic:** systolic **<50 mmHg** (day 1) · **<65 mmHg** (≤1 month)

**Perfusion:** capillary refill **>3 s** · lactate **>3 mmol/L**

---

## Differential diagnosis

> NNS may **coexist with** other pathologies **and mimic** them.

- **Neurologic:** HIE, neonatal seizure, intracranial haemorrhage
- **Respiratory:** RDS, aspiration pneumonia, TOF, TTN
- **Cardiovascular:** hypoplastic left heart, PPHN, myocarditis, shock
- **GIT:** **NEC**, spontaneous GI perforation
- **Metabolic:** hypoglycaemia, adrenal disorders, CAH, inborn errors
- **Haematologic:** vitamin K deficiency, purpura fulminans, immune thrombocytopenia/neutropenia, **ABO/Rh isoimmunization, G6PD**

---

## Management principles

1. Review clinical features; **high index of suspicion**; balance risks against features
2. **Investigate and take cultures**
3. **Commence antibiotics IMMEDIATELY**, especially with major risk factors
4. **Review** with clinical progress and lab results
5. **Repeat investigations** — sudden deterioration may occur

---

## Investigations

**Sepsis screen:** FBC · **blood cultures** · CSF · urine culture · other body fluids · CXR if indicated

---

**FBC findings suggesting sepsis:**

- WBC **< 5,000** or **> 34,000/mm³**
- **Absolute neutrophil < 1,000/mm³**
- **Bands:total neutrophils > 0.2**
- **Immature:mature > 0.2**

---

**Töllner score:** **<5 normal · 5–10 suspected · >10 definite sepsis**

---

**Markers:** band:total ratio (<0.16 at 12 hr, <0.12 after 60 hr) · micro-ESR · **CRP >10 mg/L** · hs-CRP >1 mg/L · **procalcitonin >2–2.5 ng/mL after 72 hr** · **IL-6/IL-8 >70 pg/mL** · thrombocytopenia **<100,000** · immature neutrophils **>10%** · **16S PCR**

---

**Critical fact:**

> **Blood culture is positive in only 5–10% of neonatal sepsis.**

So a negative culture **does not** exclude sepsis. That is why "clinical", "probable" and "possible" sepsis categories exist at all.

---

**Lumbar puncture — three indications:** primary diagnosis of sepsis · positive blood culture · neurological signs

**Renal function** — because of **aminoglycosides**

---

### NOT recommended

> **Superficial swabs** (axilla, cord, ear canal, nasopharynx, orogastric tube) — **poor correlation**

> **Tracheal aspirate** in prolonged intubation — rapid colonization

> **Urine culture in EOS**

---

## Antibiotics

**Rational use:** correct quality antibiotic · right dose · wide coverage · right duration · right route · right patient

**Choice based on:** **local sensitivity pattern**, and patient response where lab support is poor

---

**WHO recommendation:**

- **First line:** penicillin (benzylpenicillin/ampicillin/amoxicillin) **+ gentamicin**, then vancomycin/cloxacillin + amikacin
- **Second line:** penicillin/cephalosporin (cefotaxime/ceftriaxone) **+ amikacin**

**Nosocomial:** vancomycin or fluoroquinolones + amikacin

---

**The resistance problem here:**

> *Klebsiella* resistance to **gentamicin 60–72%**, and to **3rd generation cephalosporins 57–66%**.

**Monitor response with:** clinical state · CRP · procalcitonin · immature/total neutrophil ratio

---

## Supportive care

Correct **anaemia, electrolytes, acid/base** · prevent **hypoxia and hypo/hyperglycaemia** · **thermoneutral environment** · **exchange or buffy coat transfusion** (sclerema, bacterial hepatitis with high conjugated bilirubin, leucopenia) · recombinant cytokines · manage **hypotension and shock**

**Doubtful use:** granulocyte transfusions · GM-CSF · IVIG

**Showed better survival:** **pentoxifylline** — reduces **TNF-α** and improves microcirculation

---

## Prevention — high yield

- Minimise risk factors
- **Intrapartum penicillin prophylaxis 2–4 hours before SVD** in GBS culture-positive mothers → significantly reduces **EOS**
- **Good ANC** — avoid preterm/LBW and asphyxia
- **Regular hand washing**
- **Eye care — 0.5% erythromycin eye ointment stat**
- **Cord care — 4% chlorhexidine gel once daily for 5–7 days**
- **Skin-to-skin on mother's abdomen for the critical first hour**
- **Early and exclusive breastfeeding**
- **Reduce traffic flow** in the newborn unit
- **Personalize facilities** for each admitted newborn
- Sepsis control in the unit
- **Rational use of antibiotics**
