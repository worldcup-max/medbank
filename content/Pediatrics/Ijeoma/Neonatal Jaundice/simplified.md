# Neonatal Jaundice — Simplified

**One line:** the baby turns yellow because bilirubin has built up in the blood, in the first month of life.

---

## Where the bilirubin comes from

Old red cells break down → haem → **bilirubin**. Newborns are set up to pile it up:

- Their red cells only live **under 90 days** (adults: 120) and they have more red cells per kg
- Their liver is immature — **poor uptake** (low ligandin / Y-protein) and poor conjugation
- Their gut **sends it back into the blood** — high beta-glucuronidase, no bacteria yet, slow gut

Those three lines *are* physiological jaundice. Memorise them as **make too much → clear too little → reabsorb what you did clear**.

---

## The single most important split

**Unconjugated** (indirect) — can cross into the brain → **kernicterus**. This is the one that kills or disables.

**Conjugated** (direct) — cannot hurt the brain, **but** it means the liver or bile ducts are in trouble (biliary atresia, hepatitis, TORCH). So it's a different kind of emergency.

Colour hint on exam: **bright yellow/orange** = unconjugated · **greenish/muddy yellow** = conjugated.

---

## Sorting out the cause

**Unconjugated + haemolysis**

- **Coombs positive** → ABO or Rh incompatibility
- **Coombs negative** → G6PD, spherocytosis, haemoglobinopathy, sepsis, DIC, haematoma, polycythaemia
- Drugs: diazepam, oxytocin

**Unconjugated, no haemolysis** → breast milk jaundice, Crigler–Najjar I & II, Gilbert, hypothyroidism

**Conjugated**

- Liver problem → neonatal hepatitis, Dubin–Johnson, Rotor, TORCH/sepsis, galactosaemia, tyrosinaemia
- Blockage after the liver → **biliary atresia**, bile duct stenosis, choledochal cyst

**In our environment, the usual six:** physiological · blood group incompatibility · G6PD · breast milk jaundice · sepsis · cephalhaematoma.

---

## Looking at the baby

Yellow spreads **head → toe**. That's **Kramer's index**:

| Where you see it | Roughly how high |
|---|---|
| Face | 4–6 |
| Chest / upper trunk | 8–10 |
| Lower abdomen, thigh | 12–14 |
| Forearms, lower legs | 15–18 |
| **Palms & soles** | **> 15–20** |

Palms and soles yellow = **very high**. But never trust your eyes alone — measure it.

---

## Breast milk vs breastfeeding jaundice

Easy to mix up. The trick is **when** and **why**:

- **Breastfeeding jaundice** — *within the first week*, because the baby isn't feeding enough (dehydrated, low calories). Fix: **feed more often**. 13% of babies.
- **Breast milk jaundice** — *after day 7*, peaks at 2–3 weeks, from glucuronidase in the milk. Fix: phototherapy; level falls if breastfeeding stops. 2% of babies.

Remember: **feeding problem = early; milk problem = late.**

---

## Treatment — three tools

**1. Phototherapy** — blue light, **420–470 nm**. Turns bilirubin into water-soluble forms (lumirubin, photoisomers) that leave *without* needing the liver. Should drop the level **1–2 mg/dL in 4–6 hours**.

- Start at: **>15 term**, **>12 preterm**, **>5 in the first 24 hours**
- 45 cm away (intensive: 15–20 cm), cover **eyes and genitals**, turn baby every 2–4 hours, extra fluids
- Don't use in: porphyria, conjugated jaundice (→ **bronze baby**), ≥20 mg/dL, light hypersensitivity

**2. Exchange blood transfusion** — when it's dangerous *now*: **≥20 term**, **≥15 LBW preterm**, rising 5 mg/dL/day, >10 on day 1, or **any sign of kernicterus**. Push–pull through the umbilical vein, **double the blood volume** (160–170 mL/kg) over 60–90 min. Calcium gluconate 1 mL of 10% after every 100 mL.

**3. Drugs** — not routine. Phenobarbitone (5–8 mg/kg/day, works in 48–72 h), IVIG for Rh/ABO, albumin, oral agar, tin mesoporphyrin.

---

## Why we bother — kernicterus

Unconjugated bilirubin stains the brain: **basal ganglia, brainstem nuclei, cerebellar nuclei, hippocampus, anterior horn cells**.

**Acute, in three phases:**

1. Days 1–3 — **floppy**: hypotonia, lethargy, high-pitched cry, poor suck, no Moro
2. Middle of week 1 — **stiff**: hypertonia, opisthotonus, retrocollis, oculogyric crisis, fever, seizures
3. After week 1 — floppy again

**Chronic:** first year floppy with brisk reflexes and delayed milestones; after that **choreoathetosis, upward gaze palsy, sensorineural deafness**.

Grim numbers: **30%** of untreated haemolytic disease with bilirubin >25–30 develop it; **>75% die**; **80% of survivors** have choreoathetosis.

---

## Conjugated jaundice treatment

Supportive only: **vitamin K**, vitamins A and D (stops rickets), **low-fat / MCT diet**, phenobarbitone to help bile flow, surgery later if needed.

---

## The bottom line

Most jaundice is harmless. Your job is to spot the dangerous patterns — **first 24 hours, rising fast, conjugated, or lasting past 14 days** — measure the level, and get it down before it reaches the brain.
