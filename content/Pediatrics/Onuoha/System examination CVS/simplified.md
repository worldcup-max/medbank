# CVS Examination — the simple version

## Start with the big idea

> A cardiovascular history is not a random list of questions. It is a **funnel** — seven questions that narrow from "is there heart disease at all?" down to "exactly which lesion is it?"

Learn the **seven questions in order** and the whole history organises itself.

---

## The seven questions — the spine of everything

**1. Are there SYMPTOMS of heart disease?**

**2. Is it CONGENITAL or ACQUIRED?**

**3. If congenital — CYANOTIC or ACYANOTIC?**

**4. If cyanotic — WHICH ONE?**

**5. If acyanotic — WHICH ONE?**

**6. Is it RHEUMATIC (fever or heart disease)?**

**7. Are there COMPLICATIONS?**

---

> Notice the shape: each answer **decides which question you ask next**. It is a decision tree, not a checklist.

---

### Question 2 made simple — congenital vs acquired

Ask **one thing: when did it start?**

- **From infancy** (feeding trouble, failure to thrive, recurrent admissions) → **CONGENITAL**
- **Started later** (fever, joint pain, chorea) → **ACQUIRED — think rheumatic fever**

---

### Question 3 made simple — cyanotic vs acyanotic

> Is the child **blue**?

- **Blue** → cyanotic CHD
- **Not blue** → acyanotic CHD

But you must know **central vs peripheral** cyanosis, because only **central** means heart or lung.

---

## Central vs peripheral cyanosis — the six-line table

The single cleanest way to remember it: **central is a whole-body problem, peripheral is a local one.**

| Ask | Central | Peripheral |
|---|---|---|
| Why? | **low arterial O₂ saturation** | **poor local blood flow** |
| Where? | **tongue, lips** (mucous membranes) | **skin only** |
| Clubbing? | **yes** | no |
| Limb warm or cold? | **warm** | **cold** |
| Warm the limb — does it clear? | **no** | **yes** |
| Give oxygen — does it improve? | **yes** | no |

---

> **The tongue is the decider.** A blue tongue is **central** cyanosis. Blue fingers with a pink tongue is **peripheral**.

---

## Questions 4 and 5 — sorting the actual defect

You do not memorise defects randomly. You sort them.

**Cyanotic CHD → sort by pulmonary blood flow:**

- **Decreased PBF** → **TOF**, tricuspid atresia, TGA with PS
- **Increased PBF** → truncus arteriosus, TAPVC

**Acyanotic CHD → sort by overload type:**

- **Volume overload (a shunt)** → **ASD, VSD, PDA**
- **Pressure overload (an obstruction)** → **PS, AS, CoA**

---

> **Shunt = volume. Obstruction = pressure.** Two words unlock the whole acyanotic list.

---

## Nada's criteria — how to say "yes, there is heart disease"

You need **1 major OR 2 minor**.

**Major (the serious findings):**

- Systolic murmur **grade III or louder**
- **Diastolic** murmur
- **Cyanosis**
- **Heart failure**

**Minor (the softer clues):**

- Systolic murmur **grade II or softer**
- Abnormal **S2**, abnormal **ECG**, abnormal **CXR**, abnormal **BP**

---

> The logic: a **loud** murmur or a **diastolic** murmur is serious on its own (major). A **soft** murmur is only meaningful if something else is also off (needs a second minor).

---

## The history clues that predict the defect

Three tables that examiners love. Do not memorise all of them — learn the **headline** in each.

**Mothers:**

- **Diabetic mother** → TGA, VSD (and HOCM)
- **SLE** → **congenital heart block**

**Drugs in pregnancy:**

- **Lithium** → **Ebstein's anomaly** (the classic pairing)
- **Alcohol** → VSD

**Syndromes:**

- **Down's** → **ECD / VSD**
- **Turner** → **CoA**
- **Marfan** → **AR / MVP**
- **Noonan** → **PS**

---

> If you only remember four: **Lithium–Ebstein, SLE–heart block, Down's–ECD, Turner–CoA.**

---

## The examination sequence — say it in order

> **Pulse → BP → JVP → Inspect → Palpate → Percuss → Auscultate.**

Same order every time. The examiner is watching for the sequence itself.

---

## Pulse — eight things, but four that matter most

You could assess eight features. The four that win marks are **rate, rhythm, volume, character.**

---

### Rate — where and how

Count the **radial** for a **full minute**.

- **Fast** → rheumatic fever, heart failure, arrhythmia
- **Slow** → **complete heart block**, sick sinus syndrome

---

### Rhythm — three patterns

- **Regular** → normal
- **Regularly irregular** → sinus arrhythmia
- **IRREGULARLY irregular** → **atrial fibrillation**

> "Irregularly irregular" is a buzz-phrase. It means **AF** until proven otherwise.

---

### Volume — measured by pulse pressure

Pulse pressure = systolic − diastolic. Normal **30–60**.

- **> 60 → bounding** (AR, PDA)
- **< 30 → weak, thready** (CCF, pericardial effusion)

---

### Character — the waveforms worth naming

Give each a one-line hook:

- **Slow-rising (parvus et tardus)** → **aortic STENOSIS** (slow to rise, low peak)
- **Collapsing** → **aortic REGURGITATION** (rapid up, rapid down)
- **Pulsus alternans** (big-small-big-small) → **severe LV failure**
- **Pulsus paradoxus** (BP drops > 10 on inspiration) → **cardiac TAMPONADE**

---

> **AS and AR are opposites.** Stenosis = slow and small. Regurgitation = fast and collapsing. Remember one and the other is its mirror image.

---

### Two delays to check

- **Radio-femoral delay** → **coarctation of the aorta**
- **Pulse deficit** (HR faster than PR) → **atrial fibrillation**

---

## Blood pressure — the cuff size fact

> **Width = 40%** of the arm circumference. **Length = 80–100%.**

A cuff too small reads **falsely high**; too big reads **falsely low**. In children the wrong cuff is the commonest cause of a wrong reading.

---

## JVP — one formula, one number

> **Right atrial pressure = height of the column + 5 cm** (the RA sits ~5 cm below the sternal angle).

> **Normal JVP = under 8 cm H₂O.**

Patient at **45°**, look at the **internal jugular**.

- **Raised** → CCF, tricuspid disease, constrictive pericarditis, tamponade
- **Kussmaul's sign** (JVP rises on inspiration — the wrong way) → constrictive pericarditis, tamponade

---

## Palpation — which part of the hand?

A neat, examinable rule:

> **Fingertips** feel **pulsations.**
> **Base of fingers** feels **thrills.**
> **Ulnar/heel of hand** feels **heaves.**

---

### The apex beat — normal position by age

| Age | Where |
|---|---|
| Infant | **4th** space, **lateral** to MCL |
| ~5 years | **5th** space, **in** the MCL |
| Older | **5th** space, **medial** to MCL |

> As the child grows, the apex moves **down, in, and medial**. Displaced **out and down** = enlargement.

---

### Two apex types that mean opposite things

- **HEAVING** (sustained, forceful) → **pressure** overload → **hypertrophy** (AS, HTN)
- **HYPERDYNAMIC** (diffuse, thrusting) → **volume** overload → **dilatation** (AR, MR, VSD)

> **Heaving = pressure = hypertrophy. Hyperdynamic = volume = dilatation.**

---

### Thrill — what it actually means

> A **thrill is a palpable murmur.** If you can **feel** it, the murmur is **at least grade 4/6.**

---

## Auscultation — build it up in layers

Heart sounds are made by **valves closing.**

- **S1** = mitral + tricuspid close (**start** of systole)
- **S2** = aortic + pulmonary close (**end** of systole)

---

### The four areas — where each valve is loudest

- **Aortic** — 2nd space, **right** sternal border
- **Pulmonary** — 2nd space, **left** sternal border
- **Tricuspid** — left **lower** sternal border
- **Mitral** — **apex** (5th space, mid-clavicular line)

---

### Splitting of S2 — understand it, don't cram it

Normal: S2 **splits on inspiration** and comes back together on expiration.

**Why?** Inspiration → negative chest pressure → **more blood into the right heart** → RV takes **longer** to empty → pulmonary valve closes **later** → you hear two sounds.

Now the two exam answers:

- **ASD → FIXED split** (does not move with breathing), because the shunt keeps RV volume high all the time
- **PS → WIDE split**, because the RV struggles through the narrow valve

---

### Murmurs — one idea, then sort by timing

> A murmur is just **turbulent blood** — either too much flow, or flow through a narrowed hole.

**Sort by WHEN in the cycle:**

| Timing | Classic causes |
|---|---|
| **Pan-systolic** | **MR, TR, VSD** |
| **Ejection-systolic** | **AS, PS, HOCM** |
| **Early diastolic** | **AR, PR** |
| **Mid-diastolic** | **MS** |
| **Continuous** | **PDA** |

---

> **Pan-systolic = "leaks and holes"** (regurgitations + VSD). **Ejection-systolic = "tight valves"** (stenoses). That split covers most of them.

---

### Grading in one line

> **Grade 4 and above = you can FEEL a thrill.** Below 4, you cannot.

---

## Innocent murmurs — the seven S's

A benign murmur every S you can think of:

> **Soft · Single · Short · Sweet · Systolic · Symptomless · Sensitive** (changes with position).

Plus: **under grade 3**, normal pulses, normal heart sounds, normal CXR, **no cyanosis**.

---

### When is a murmur NOT innocent? (refer)

- **Louder than 2/6**
- **DIASTOLIC** (any diastolic murmur is abnormal)
- **Louder on standing**
- **Any symptoms** — failure to thrive, feeding difficulty, cyanosis, breathlessness

---

> **Every diastolic murmur is pathological.** No diastolic murmur is ever innocent. Remember that one.

---

## Dynamic auscultation — how to make a murmur move

You change conditions and see which murmur responds.

**Breathing (the RILE rule):**

> **RI**ght-sided murmurs louder on **I**nspiration; **LE**ft-sided louder on **E**xpiration.

**The two special cases that get LOUDER on Valsalva/standing:**

- **HOCM**
- **Mitral valve prolapse**

> Almost every murmur gets **softer** when you reduce venous return (Valsalva, standing). **HOCM and MVP are the rebels — they get louder.** That is exactly why examiners love them.

---

## Putting it all together

The history is a **seven-question funnel**: symptoms → congenital or acquired → cyanotic or acyanotic → which defect → rheumatic? → complications.

**Central cyanosis** (blue tongue, clears with oxygen) means heart or lung; **peripheral** does not.

The examination runs **pulse → BP → JVP → inspect → palpate → percuss → auscultate**, always in that order.

Two pattern-pairs carry a huge amount of the marks: **heaving = pressure = hypertrophy** vs **hyperdynamic = volume = dilatation**, and **ejection-systolic = tight valves (stenosis)** vs **pan-systolic = leaks and holes (regurgitation/VSD)**.

And the two rules that are almost always tested: **every diastolic murmur is abnormal**, and **HOCM and MVP get louder when everything else gets softer.**
