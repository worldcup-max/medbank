# Seizure Disorders — the simple version

## Start by separating three words people mix up

These three are NOT the same thing, and the exam loves the difference:

> **SEIZURE** = a single event. One electrical storm in the brain.
>
> **EPILEPSY** = a tendency to have them **again and again, unprovoked**.
>
> **SEIZURE DISORDER** = the umbrella word covering both, plus febrile seizures.

---

**The key line to hold:**

> A seizure is caused by **excessive and hypersynchronous discharge of cortical neurons.**

"Hypersynchronous" = the neurons that should fire **independently** suddenly all fire **together**, like a crowd stamping in unison.

---

## Provoked vs unprovoked — the single most useful split

> **PROVOKED** — there is a **trigger**. Remove the trigger, the seizure stops. (fever, low sugar, infection)
>
> **UNPROVOKED** — **no trigger**. The brain does it on its own.

**Why this matters:**

> **Epilepsy = recurrent UNPROVOKED seizures.**

A child who fits only when feverish does **not** have epilepsy — they have **provoked** (febrile) seizures. This distinction decides whether you label a child "epileptic" for life.

---

## Genetic vs structural — the old names are the clue

The names changed, but examiners use both:

| New name | Old name | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **Genetic** | **Idiopathic** | no cause found, **normal brain, normal IQ** |
| **Structural** | **Symptomatic** | a **structural brain problem** is causing it |

> **Genetic = normal child, normal scan. Structural = something is physically wrong in the brain.**

---

## The numbers to know

- Seizures are the **commonest neurological problem** in children
- **10% of children** have a seizure — but **fewer than 1/3** are epilepsy
- **Lifetime incidence of epilepsy = 3%**
- More common in **early childhood**, in **boys** (1.5:1), and in **poorer families**
- **Resource-poor countries** have far higher rates (100–190 vs 40–70 per 100,000)

> The gap between "10% have a seizure" and "3% get epilepsy" tells the whole story: **most childhood seizures are one-off or provoked, not epilepsy.**

---

## Classification — the 2017 system in one picture

Ask **ONE question first: where did it start?**

> **FOCAL** = starts in **one part of one hemisphere**
>
> **GENERALIZED** = starts in **both hemispheres at once**
>
> **UNKNOWN** = you cannot tell
>
> **UNCLASSIFIED** = odd features, cannot decide even after workup

---

**Then for focal seizures, ask a second question: is awareness kept?**

- **Aware** (old "simple partial")
- **Impaired awareness** (old "complex partial")

> **Focal + aware = simple. Focal + awareness lost = complex.** That is the whole old-to-new translation.

---

## Aetiology — the one sentence that reframes everything

> **A seizure is a SYMPTOM, not a diagnosis.**

So your job is always to ask **"a symptom of WHAT?"**

Split the causes into **congenital** (born with it) and **acquired** (happened later):

**Acquired — remember the buckets:**

- **Trauma** — birth injury, asphyxia
- **Infection** — meningitis, encephalitis, cerebral malaria
- **Metabolic** — low/high Na, low Ca, Mg, glucose
- **Toxic** — lead, drugs (including withdrawal)
- **Structural** — tumour, stroke, AVM
- **Febrile**

> In a child who fits, **always check glucose, sodium, calcium** and think **infection** — these are the **treatable** causes you must not miss.

---

## Pathophysiology — a see-saw

Two chemicals, pulling opposite ways:

> **GLUTAMATE** (and aspartate) = the **accelerator** (excitation)
>
> **GABA** = the **brake** (inhibition)

A seizure happens when the see-saw tips toward excitation:

- **Too much accelerator** — over-active glutamate, OR
- **Failed brake** — not enough GABA, OR
- **Both**

> This is why the drugs make sense: most anti-seizure drugs either **cut the accelerator** (block Na⁺ channels) or **boost the brake** (enhance GABA).

---

## The clinical types — match each to its EEG

The EEG pattern is a favourite OSPE image. Learn each type WITH its trace.

---

### Generalized tonic-clonic (GTCS) — the commonest

Two phases:

> **TONIC** — stiff, sustained contraction (the body arches)
>
> **CLONIC** — rhythmic jerking (contract–relax–contract–relax)

Plus loss of consciousness, and autonomic effects (incontinence, cyanosis, apnoea).

**EEG:** fast **spikes/polyspikes at 10–15 Hz** in the tonic phase, then **slow waves** in the clonic phase.

---

### Absence seizure — the "blank stare"

> Child suddenly **stops, stares blankly**, lasts **< 30 sec**, then **carries on as if nothing happened**.

- **No falling, no postictal drowsiness** — that is the giveaway
- Age **~5 years**, more in **girls**, can happen **countless times a day**

**EEG — memorise this number:**

> **3 per second (3 Hz) spike-and-wave.**

> Teachers often report the child as "**daydreaming**" or "not paying attention." That is the classic history.

---

### Infantile spasms — the dangerous one

- **Baby aged 4–8 months**
- Sudden brief **< 1 sec** spasms, **commonest type is mixed** (flexion + extension), in **clusters**
- **80–90% end up with mental retardation** — this is serious

**EEG — the buzzword:**

> **HYPSARRHYTHMIA** — a chaotic, high-voltage, disorganized trace.

**West syndrome = the triad:**

> **Infantile Spasms + Mental retardation + Hypsarrhythmia.**

---

### Rolandic epilepsy (benign, centrotemporal spikes)

- **School-age child**, fits **at night**, **remits in adolescence** — genuinely benign
- **Drug of choice: carbamazepine**
- **EEG:** centrotemporal spikes on a **normal background**

---

## A quick way to hold the four EEGs

| Seizure | EEG buzzword |
|---|---|
| **Absence** | **3 Hz spike-and-wave** |
| **Infantile spasm** | **hypsarrhythmia** |
| **GTCS** | spikes/polyspikes 10–15 Hz then slow waves |
| **Rolandic** | centrotemporal spikes, normal background |

---

## Diagnosis — one golden rule

> **The diagnosis of epilepsy is CLINICAL.**

It rests on a **good description of the event** — from the child or, better, an **eyewitness**. The EEG supports; it does not replace the story.

**Investigations back it up:** glucose, electrolytes (with Ca and Mg), EEG (awake and asleep), neuroimaging if focal signs or raised ICP, and **LP if infection is suspected — provided there are no contraindications.**

---

## Seizure mimics — things that LOOK like fits but aren't

The exam wants you to know a fit can be imitated:

- **Breath-holding spells** (toddlers)
- **Syncope** — can look like an atonic seizure
- **Night terrors, sleep-walking** (parasomnias)
- **Prolonged QT syndrome** — collapse **on exercise/emotion** (a cardiac cause — do an ECG!)
- **Pseudoseizures (PNES)** — psychological, not electrical

> **Always do an ECG** if collapse happens on exertion — **long QT** can kill and is mistaken for epilepsy.

---

## Treatment — think in steps

> **Step 1: ABC** (resuscitate first — always)
>
> **Step 2: Abort** the seizure
>
> **Step 3: Evaluate** for the cause
>
> **Step 4: Treat** the cause
>
> **Step 5: Long-term** ASM + neurology follow-up

---

### Matching the drug to the seizure — the high-yield pairs

| Seizure | Drug |
|---|---|
| **Typical absence** | **ethosuximide** (or valproate) |
| **Infantile spasms** | **ACTH** |
| **Rolandic** | **carbamazepine** |
| **Broad spectrum (many types)** | **sodium valproate** |
| **Status epilepticus (first line)** | **benzodiazepine** |

---

### Two drug side-effects examiners love

> **Phenytoin → GUM HYPERTROPHY** (also acne, ataxia, nystagmus)
>
> **Carbamazepine → same mechanism as phenytoin** (blocks Na⁺ channels)

---

## Status epilepticus — the emergency

> **Old:** a seizure > 30 minutes (or repeated without recovery).
>
> **New (use this):** a seizure **> 5 minutes**, or repeated seizures within 5 minutes **without recovering consciousness**.

**Why did the definition shrink to 5 minutes?**

> Because most seizures stop by themselves within 2–3 minutes. One lasting **5 minutes** will probably **not** stop on its own and needs drugs **now**. Waiting 30 minutes causes brain injury.

---

### The status ladder

> **First line: BENZODIAZEPINE** (diazepam, lorazepam, or buccal/nasal midazolam if no IV)
>
> **Second line: phenytoin / fosphenytoin, phenobarbital, valproate, levetiracetam**
>
> **Third line: ICU — anaesthesia (thiopental, midazolam, propofol), intubate, ventilate**

Watch the benzodiazepine side effect: **respiratory depression.**

---

## Febrile seizures — the one you'll see most

Nail the definition exactly:

> Age **6–60 months** (peak 12–18), temp **≥ 38°C**, **NO CNS infection**, **NO metabolic cause**, **NO prior afebrile seizure**.

Every clause matters — if there IS a CNS infection, it is **not** a febrile seizure, it is meningitis.

---

### Simple vs complex — three questions

| | Simple | Complex |
|---|---|---|
| How long? | **< 15 min** | **> 15 min** |
| How many in 24h? | **one** | **multiple** |
| Focal or general? | **generalized** | **focal** |

> **Simple = short, single, generalized. Complex = the opposite of each.**

---

### The rule that saves lives

> **Before you call it a febrile seizure, RULE OUT meningitis.**

And specifically:

> Do an **LP in a child under 18 months**, because at that age meningitis may **not** show the usual neck stiffness.

---

### The reassuring number

> After a **simple** febrile seizure, the risk of later epilepsy is only **1%** — barely above the general population.

The risk climbs with **complex/focal** seizures, **family history of epilepsy**, and **neurodevelopmental abnormality** (up to 33%).

---

## Putting it all together

**Three words, kept separate:** a **seizure** is one event, **epilepsy** is recurrent **unprovoked** seizures, **seizure disorder** is the umbrella.

Classify by **where it starts** (focal vs generalized), and for focal, **whether awareness is kept**.

A seizure is a **symptom** — always hunt the cause, especially the treatable ones (glucose, Na, Ca, infection).

The pathophysiology is a **see-saw**: too much **glutamate** or too little **GABA** — which is exactly what the drugs target.

Match the **EEG buzzwords** (3 Hz absence, hypsarrhythmia = infantile spasms/West) and the **drug pairs** (ethosuximide-absence, ACTH-spasms, carbamazepine-Rolandic, benzo-status).

And for the everyday one — **febrile seizures**: get the definition exact, split **simple vs complex**, and **always exclude meningitis**, with an **LP under 18 months**.
