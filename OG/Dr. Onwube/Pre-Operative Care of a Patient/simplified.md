# Pre-Operative Care — Simplified

**One line:** everything you do *before* the knife, to make sure the patient survives the operation and recovers well.

---

## The whole point

Get the patient into the **best possible condition** — physically *and* psychologically — before surgery.

Two goals:

- Successful outcome from the operation
- Satisfactory recovery afterwards, with fewer short and long term complications

And it is a **team** job — not just the surgeon.

---

## Six ways to classify a gynae operation

Each one changes how you prepare:

- Elective or **Emergency**
- Major or Minor
- Day case or in-patient
- Abdominal or Vaginal
- Diagnostic or Therapeutic
- Minimal access surgery

---

## The two most important things you will do

**History and physical examination.**

Not the tests. The tests come *from* the history and examination.

They dictate what else is needed.

---

## What you're looking for, system by system

| System | Looking for | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| General | Pallor, jaundice | Anaemia; liver disease |
| CVS | Pulse, BP, heart sounds, oedema | **Tachycardia is the first sign of CV compromise** — key in ectopic |
| Respiratory | Infection, TB, asthma | May postpone surgery or change anaesthetic |
| GIT | Appendicitis mimicking ectopic; liver disease | Liver disease affects anaesthetic metabolism |
| Urinary | UTI; obstructive uropathy from fibroids | |
| Endocrine | Diabetes, thyroid disease | Must be excluded |

---

## Investigations — do not order everything

> Avoid a battery of investigations routinely. It only increases cost.

**Everyone gets:** Hb concentration. Minimum **10 g/dL** for elective surgery.

**But in an emergency** — ruptured ectopic — a low Hb does **not** stop you operating. The aim is to stop the bleeding.

**Others, when indicated:**

- **Platelets** — missed abortion, coagulation problems
- **Hb genotype** — anaesthesia can trigger sickling crisis
- **Group & cross-match** — when heavy blood loss is likely
- **USS** — exclude pregnancy or obstructive uropathy
- **CXR** — hypertension, cardiac or respiratory problems; routine over **50**
- **ECG** — over **60**, hypertensive, or known cardiac disease

Remember the numbers: **CXR at 50, ECG at 60.**

---

## Admission — keep it short

Long pre-op admission is bad for:

- The patient's mind
- Her money
- Bed space for someone who actually needs it

**On admission, re-evaluate:**

- Clerk again in detail
- Check investigations are complete
- Confirm diagnosis and intended operation

Then ask five questions:

1. Has anything changed?
2. Any intercurrent illness?
3. Is the procedure still necessary?
4. Is it the best option for her?
5. Any additional procedure to do at the same time?

---

## Consent

**Without duress.** In words she understands.

Cover: the procedure, the indication, the alternatives, the expected outcome, benefits and complications.

But — do **not** terrify her out of an operation that will help her.

- She signs it herself; a minor's is signed by parent/guardian
- Make it **non-restrictive**, so extra procedures can be done if needed during surgery

---

## Operation list

Who is operating, and in what order.

Agreed with the **anaesthetist** and sent to theatre **well in advance** — so they can arrange special equipment.

---

## The anaesthetist's visit

The anaesthetist is the **final clinical gatekeeper**.

They: build rapport, allay anxiety, prescribe the anaesthetic and the premedication.

**NPO:** traditionally **8 hours** — empty stomach, no regurgitation or aspiration.

But don't overdo it: prolonged fasting causes **hypoglycaemia**. Newer guidelines push for no more than **6 hours**.

---

## Getting the patient ready

**Do:**

- Remove hairpieces and elaborate hairstyles
- Morning bath
- Remove **nail polish** (blocks the pulse oximeter)
- Wash off **lipstick** (obscures assessment)
- Hair removal **only** at the incision site, **shortly before** surgery — not the night before

**Don't do routinely:**

- Bowel preparation — only if bowel surgery is expected
- Vaginal cleansing — shown **not** to be useful

**Do in theatre, under anaesthesia:** bladder catheterisation.

And a good night's sleep with friendly nursing staff is genuinely part of the preparation.

---

## Prophylaxis — two kinds

**Antibiotics:** giving them **before** surgery is more cost-effective than seven days of treatment afterwards. Choose by local sensitivity patterns.

**DVT:** assess the risk, then —

- Adequate hydration
- Graduated compression stockings
- Heparin

And note: **prolonged pre-operative bed rest itself raises the risk** of post-op thromboembolism. Another reason not to admit early.
